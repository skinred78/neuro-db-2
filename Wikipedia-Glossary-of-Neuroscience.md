## A

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: A")\]

[Acetylcholine](/wiki/Acetylcholine "Acetylcholine")

A neurotransmitter involved in muscle activation, attention, arousal, and memory. It acts at both nicotinic and muscarinic receptors throughout the central and peripheral nervous systems.

[Achromatopsia](/wiki/Achromatopsia "Achromatopsia")

Loss of color vision as a result of damage to extrastriate visual cortex.

[Action Potential](/wiki/Action_Potential "Action Potential")

A rapid electrical signal that travels down the axon of a neuron.

[Activation](/wiki/Activation "Activation")

The time-dependent opening of ion channels in response to a stimulus, typically membrane depolarization.

[Adaptation](/wiki/Adaptation "Adaptation")

The phenomenon of sensory receptor adjustment to different levels of stimulation; critical for allowing sensory systems to operate over a wide dynamic range.

[Adenylyl cyclase](/wiki/Adenylyl_cyclase "Adenylyl cyclase")

Membrane-bound enzyme that can be activated by G-proteins to catalyze the synthesis of cyclic AMP from ATP.

[Adrenal cortex](/wiki/Adrenal_cortex "Adrenal cortex")

The outer region of the adrenal gland, responsible for producing steroid hormones such as cortisol and aldosterone, which regulate metabolism and stress responses.

[Adrenal medulla](/wiki/Adrenal_medulla "Adrenal medulla")

The central part of the adrenal gland that, under visceral motor stimulation, secretes epinephrine and norepinephrine into the bloodstream.

[Adrenaline](/wiki/Adrenaline "Adrenaline")

See [epinephrine](/wiki/Epinephrine "Epinephrine").

[Afferent nerve fiber](/wiki/Afferent_nerve_fiber "Afferent nerve fiber")

An axon that conducts action potentials from the periphery toward the central nervous system.

[Agnosia](/wiki/Agnosia "Agnosia")

The inability to name objects, typically resulting from brain damage in the occipital or temporal lobes.

[Agonist](/wiki/Agonist "Agonist")

A chemical that binds to and activates a receptor, mimicking the action of a natural neurotransmitter.

[Akinesia](/wiki/Akinesia "Akinesia")

A loss or impairment of voluntary movement, often associated with [Parkinson's disease](/wiki/Parkinson%27s_disease "Parkinson's disease") and basal ganglia dysfunction.

[Allodynia](/wiki/Allodynia "Allodynia")

A condition in which normally non-painful stimuli are perceived as painful, often due to nerve damage or sensitization.

[Alpha wave](/wiki/Alpha_wave "Alpha wave")

A type of brainwave oscillation in the frequency range of 8–13 Hz, commonly associated with relaxed, wakeful states and measured using [electroencephalography](/wiki/Electroencephalography "Electroencephalography") (EEG).

[Alzheimer's disease](/wiki/Alzheimer%27s_disease "Alzheimer's disease")

A progressive neurodegenerative disorder characterized by memory loss, cognitive decline, and the presence of amyloid plaques and neurofibrillary tangles in the brain.

[Amygdala](/wiki/Amygdala "Amygdala")

A brain structure involved in emotion, particularly fear, aggression, and emotional memory formation. It is part of the [limbic system](/wiki/Limbic_system "Limbic system").

[Anencephaly](/wiki/Anencephaly "Anencephaly")

A severe congenital condition in which a major portion of the brain, skull, and scalp is missing due to failed closure of the neural tube.

[Anhedonia](/wiki/Anhedonia "Anhedonia")

The inability to experience pleasure, commonly associated with depression and certain neurological disorders.

[Anterograde amnesia](/wiki/Anterograde_amnesia "Anterograde amnesia")

A form of memory loss in which new memories cannot be formed following brain injury or trauma; often associated with damage to the [hippocampus](/wiki/Hippocampus "Hippocampus").

[Anterior cingulate cortex](/wiki/Anterior_cingulate_cortex "Anterior cingulate cortex")

A region of the [cingulate cortex](/wiki/Cingulate_cortex "Cingulate cortex") implicated in functions such as emotion regulation, cognitive control, error detection, and decision-making.

[Aphasia](/wiki/Aphasia "Aphasia")

A language disorder resulting from brain damage, most often in the left hemisphere, that affects the production or comprehension of speech and writing.

[Apoptosis](/wiki/Apoptosis "Apoptosis")

A form of programmed cell death involved in development and disease. In the nervous system, apoptosis shapes neural circuits and removes damaged neurons.

[Arachnoid mater](/wiki/Arachnoid_mater "Arachnoid mater")

The middle of the three layers of the [meninges](/wiki/Meninges "Meninges") that surround the brain and spinal cord, located between the [dura mater](/wiki/Dura_mater "Dura mater") and the [pia mater](/wiki/Pia_mater "Pia mater").

[Arousal](/wiki/Arousal "Arousal")

A physiological and psychological state of alertness and readiness, regulated by the [reticular activating system](/wiki/Reticular_activating_system "Reticular activating system") and neurotransmitters such as [norepinephrine](/wiki/Norepinephrine "Norepinephrine") and [dopamine](/wiki/Dopamine "Dopamine").

[Astrocyte](/wiki/Astrocyte "Astrocyte")

A star-shaped [glial cell](/wiki/Glial_cell "Glial cell") in the central nervous system involved in maintaining the [blood–brain barrier](/wiki/Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier "Blood–brain barrier"), regulating neurotransmitter levels, and supporting neuronal function.

[Ataxia](/wiki/Ataxia "Ataxia")

A neurological symptom characterized by a lack of coordination and balance, typically caused by damage to the [cerebellum](/wiki/Cerebellum "Cerebellum") or its connections.

[Auditory cortex](/wiki/Auditory_cortex "Auditory cortex")

A part of the [temporal lobe](/wiki/Temporal_lobe "Temporal lobe") responsible for processing auditory information, including pitch, volume, and sound localization.

[Autonomic nervous system](/wiki/Autonomic_nervous_system "Autonomic nervous system")

A division of the peripheral nervous system that controls involuntary physiological processes, including heart rate, digestion, and respiration. It is divided into the sympathetic and parasympathetic systems.

[Axon](/wiki/Axon "Axon")

A long, slender projection of a neuron that conducts [action potentials](/wiki/Action_potentials "Action potentials") away from the cell body to synaptic terminals, where neurotransmitters are released.

[Axon hillock](/wiki/Axon_hillock "Axon hillock")

The cone-shaped region of a neuron’s soma where the [axon](/wiki/Axon "Axon") originates and [action potentials](/wiki/Action_potentials "Action potentials") are typically initiated.

## B

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: B")\]

[Babinski sign](/wiki/Babinski_sign "Babinski sign")

A reflex where the big toe extends upward and the other toes fan out when the sole of the foot is stimulated. Present in infants, but indicative of damage to the corticospinal tract in adults.

[Barrel cortex](/wiki/Barrel_cortex "Barrel cortex")

A region of the [somatosensory cortex](/wiki/Somatosensory_cortex "Somatosensory cortex") in rodents that contains clusters of neurons corresponding to individual whiskers, used as a model for studying sensory processing and plasticity.

[Baroreceptor](/wiki/Baroreceptor "Baroreceptor")

A sensory nerve ending in blood vessels that detects changes in blood pressure and relays information to the brainstem for autonomic regulation.

[Basal ganglia](/wiki/Basal_ganglia "Basal ganglia")

A group of subcortical nuclei involved in motor control, procedural learning, routine behaviors or "habits", and cognition. Dysfunction is associated with [Parkinson’s disease](/wiki/Parkinson%E2%80%99s_disease "Parkinson’s disease"), [Huntington’s disease](/wiki/Huntington%E2%80%99s_disease "Huntington’s disease"), and other movement disorders.

[Behavioral neuroscience](/wiki/Behavioral_neuroscience "Behavioral neuroscience")

A subfield of neuroscience that explores how the brain affects behavior, often using techniques from biology, psychology, and physiology.

[Beta wave](/wiki/Beta_wave "Beta wave")

A type of [brain wave](/wiki/Brain_wave "Brain wave") oscillation in the frequency range of approximately 13–30 Hz, typically associated with active concentration, alertness, and cognitive engagement.

[Bilateral](/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location#Medial_and_lateral "Anatomical terms of location")

In neuroscience, refers to structures or functions that involve both sides of the brain or body. For example, bilateral activation may occur in both hemispheres during a cognitive task.

[Binocular rivalry](/wiki/Binocular_rivalry "Binocular rivalry")

A phenomenon that occurs when two different images are presented to each eye, and perception alternates between them. It is used to study visual awareness and consciousness.

[Bipolar cell](/wiki/Bipolar_cell "Bipolar cell")

A type of [amacrine cell](/wiki/Amacrine_cell "Amacrine cell") that transmits signals from photoreceptors (rods and cones) to [ganglion cells](/wiki/Ganglion_cells "Ganglion cells") in the eye, playing a key role in visual processing.

[Bipolar disorder](/wiki/Bipolar_disorder "Bipolar disorder")

A mood disorder characterized by alternating episodes of depression and mania, associated with dysregulation in neurotransmitter systems and structural brain changes.

[Blood–brain barrier](/wiki/Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier "Blood–brain barrier") (BBB)

A selective barrier formed by endothelial cells lining brain capillaries that restricts the passage of substances from the bloodstream into the central nervous system, protecting the brain from pathogens and toxins.

[Bouton](/wiki/Axon_terminal "Axon terminal")

Also known as an axon terminal or synaptic bouton, this is the swollen end of an axon where neurotransmitters are released into the synaptic cleft.

[Brain](/wiki/Brain "Brain")

The central organ of the nervous system, responsible for processing sensory information, regulating bodily functions, and enabling thought, emotion, memory, and consciousness.

[Brain-derived neurotrophic factor](/wiki/Brain-derived_neurotrophic_factor "Brain-derived neurotrophic factor") (BDNF)

A protein that supports the survival of existing neurons and encourages the growth and differentiation of new neurons and synapses. It is crucial for long-term memory and synaptic plasticity.

[Brain plasticity](/wiki/Brain_plasticity "Brain plasticity")

Also called neuroplasticity, this refers to the brain’s ability to reorganize and adapt by forming new neural connections throughout life, especially after injury or in response to learning and experience.

[Brainstem](/wiki/Brainstem "Brainstem")

The posterior part of the brain that connects the cerebrum with the spinal cord. It includes the midbrain, pons, and medulla oblongata and regulates essential functions such as breathing, heart rate, and sleep.

[Brain ventricles](/wiki/Brain_ventricles "Brain ventricles")

A set of interconnected cavities within the brain that produce and circulate [cerebrospinal fluid](/wiki/Cerebrospinal_fluid "Cerebrospinal fluid") (CSF). Includes the lateral, third, and fourth ventricles.

[Brain waves](/wiki/Brain_waves "Brain waves")

Patterns of electrical activity in the brain, typically detected by [electroencephalography](/wiki/Electroencephalography "Electroencephalography") (EEG). Brain waves are categorized by frequency, such as alpha, beta, delta, and theta waves.

[Brain-computer interface](/wiki/Brain-computer_interface "Brain-computer interface") (BCI)

A technology that enables direct communication between the brain and an external device, often used in research, prosthetics, and communication aids for individuals with disabilities.

[Bradykinesia](/wiki/Bradykinesia "Bradykinesia")

Slowness of movement, often a symptom of [Parkinson’s disease](/wiki/Parkinson%E2%80%99s_disease "Parkinson’s disease") and other disorders involving the [basal ganglia](/wiki/Basal_ganglia "Basal ganglia").

[Broca's area](/wiki/Broca%27s_area "Broca's area")

A region in the frontal lobe, typically in the left hemisphere, associated with the production of speech. Damage can result in [Broca's aphasia](/wiki/Broca%27s_aphasia "Broca's aphasia"), characterized by impaired speech fluency.

[Broca's aphasia](/wiki/Broca%27s_aphasia "Broca's aphasia")

A language disorder caused by damage to [Broca's area](/wiki/Broca%27s_area "Broca's area"), resulting in slow, laborious speech with relatively preserved comprehension.

[Brodmann areas](/wiki/Brodmann_areas "Brodmann areas")

Regions of the cerebral cortex defined by Korbinian Brodmann based on cytoarchitecture. These areas are still widely used to describe functional brain localization (e.g., Brodmann area 17 = primary visual cortex).

[Burst firing](/wiki/Burst_firing "Burst firing")

A pattern of rapid spikes from a neuron followed by a period of quiescence. It plays a role in sensory encoding and signal amplification in various brain circuits.

## C

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: C")\]

[Calcium channel](/wiki/Calcium_channel "Calcium channel")

A type of ion channel located on the cell membrane that allows the selective entry of calcium ions (Ca²⁺) into the neuron, playing a crucial role in neurotransmitter release, gene expression, and synaptic plasticity.

[Capsaicin](/wiki/Capsaicin "Capsaicin")

A compound found in chili peppers that activates [TRPV1](/wiki/TRPV1 "TRPV1") receptors on sensory neurons, leading to the sensation of heat or burning. It is used in research and in topical analgesics for chronic pain.

[Caudate nucleus](/wiki/Caudate_nucleus "Caudate nucleus")

A C-shaped structure located in the basal ganglia, involved in motor control, learning, memory, and reward processing. It works closely with the [putamen](/wiki/Putamen "Putamen") and [globus pallidus](/wiki/Globus_pallidus "Globus pallidus").

[Central nervous system](/wiki/Central_nervous_system "Central nervous system") (CNS)

Consists of the [brain](/wiki/Brain "Brain") and [spinal cord](/wiki/Spinal_cord "Spinal cord"), responsible for processing and coordinating sensory data and motor commands.

[Cerebellum](/wiki/Cerebellum "Cerebellum")

A brain structure located at the back of the skull that plays a central role in motor coordination, balance, posture, and some cognitive functions such as attention and language.

[Cerebral cortex](/wiki/Cerebral_cortex "Cerebral cortex")

The outermost layer of the [cerebrum](/wiki/Cerebrum "Cerebrum"), composed of gray matter. It is involved in higher-order brain functions such as sensory perception, language, decision-making, and voluntary motor control.

[Cerebral hemisphere](/wiki/Cerebral_hemisphere "Cerebral hemisphere")

One of the two halves of the [cerebrum](/wiki/Cerebrum "Cerebrum"), divided into left and right hemispheres. Each hemisphere specializes in different functions but communicates via the [corpus callosum](/wiki/Corpus_callosum "Corpus callosum").

[Cerebrospinal fluid](/wiki/Cerebrospinal_fluid "Cerebrospinal fluid") (CSF)

A clear fluid found in the brain and spinal cord that cushions neural tissue, removes waste, and provides nutrients. It circulates through the [ventricular system](/wiki/Ventricular_system "Ventricular system") and subarachnoid space.

[Chemoreceptor](/wiki/Chemoreceptor "Chemoreceptor")

A sensory receptor that responds to chemical stimuli, such as taste molecules or blood oxygen levels. Located in tissues including the tongue, nasal cavity, and blood vessels.

[Cholinergic](/wiki/Cholinergic "Cholinergic")

Refers to cells or systems that use [acetylcholine](/wiki/Acetylcholine "Acetylcholine") as a neurotransmitter. Cholinergic neurons are involved in memory, attention, and muscle activation.

[Choroid plexus](/wiki/Choroid_plexus "Choroid plexus")

A network of cells located in the ventricles of the brain that produces [cerebrospinal fluid](/wiki/Cerebrospinal_fluid "Cerebrospinal fluid") and contributes to the maintenance of the blood–CSF barrier.

[Chromatolysis](/wiki/Chromatolysis "Chromatolysis")

A cellular response to neuronal injury, characterized by swelling of the cell body and dispersal of Nissl substance, indicating a disruption in protein synthesis.

[Cingulate cortex](/wiki/Cingulate_cortex "Cingulate cortex")

A part of the [limbic system](/wiki/Limbic_system "Limbic system") located on the medial side of the cerebral hemispheres. It is involved in emotion formation, pain perception, decision-making, and cognitive control.

[Cognition](/wiki/Cognition "Cognition")

The set of mental processes involved in acquiring knowledge and understanding through thought, experience, and the senses. Includes attention, memory, language, and problem-solving.

[Cognitive neuroscience](/wiki/Cognitive_neuroscience "Cognitive neuroscience")

A field that studies the biological processes underlying cognition, focusing on how brain structure and function give rise to mental activities.

[Commissure](/wiki/Commissure "Commissure")

A bundle of nerve fibers that connects corresponding areas between the two hemispheres of the brain. The largest is the [corpus callosum](/wiki/Corpus_callosum "Corpus callosum").

[Conditioned stimulus](/wiki/Conditioned_stimulus "Conditioned stimulus")

In classical conditioning, a previously neutral stimulus that, after being paired with an unconditioned stimulus, elicits a learned response.

[Conduction velocity](/wiki/Conduction_velocity "Conduction velocity")

The speed at which an [action potential](/wiki/Action_potential "Action potential") travels along a nerve fiber. It is influenced by factors such as axon diameter and myelination.

[Consciousness](/wiki/Consciousness "Consciousness")

A state of awareness of self and the environment. In neuroscience, it involves the coordinated activity of multiple brain regions, particularly the thalamocortical system.

[Corpus callosum](/wiki/Corpus_callosum "Corpus callosum")

The largest white matter structure in the brain, consisting of a thick band of axons that connects the left and right cerebral hemispheres.

[Cortical column](/wiki/Cortical_column "Cortical column")

A vertically organized group of neurons in the [cerebral cortex](/wiki/Cerebral_cortex "Cerebral cortex") that processes the same type of information, such as orientation or location in space. It is a basic functional unit of the cortex.

[Cortical plasticity](/wiki/Cortical_plasticity "Cortical plasticity")

The ability of the [cerebral cortex](/wiki/Cerebral_cortex "Cerebral cortex") to adapt and reorganize in response to experience, learning, or injury.

[Corticospinal tract](/wiki/Corticospinal_tract "Corticospinal tract")

A major descending pathway from the [motor cortex](/wiki/Motor_cortex "Motor cortex") to the spinal cord that mediates voluntary motor control, especially fine movements of the limbs.

[Creutzfeldt–Jakob disease](/wiki/Creutzfeldt%E2%80%93Jakob_disease "Creutzfeldt–Jakob disease") (CJD)

A rare and fatal neurodegenerative disorder caused by misfolded prion proteins. It leads to rapid cognitive decline, motor dysfunction, and ultimately death.

[Critical period](/wiki/Critical_period "Critical period")

A developmental window during which the nervous system is particularly sensitive to certain environmental stimuli, crucial for proper development of functions such as vision and language.

[Cross modal plasticity](/wiki/Cross_modal_plasticity "Cross modal plasticity")

The ability of one sensory modality to reorganize and compensate in another’s absence (e.g., visual cortex being recruited for touch in individuals who are blind).

## D

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: D")\]

[Dendrite](/wiki/Dendrite "Dendrite")

A branched projection of a neuron that receives synaptic input from other neurons and transmits electrical signals toward the cell body.

[Dendritic spine](/wiki/Dendritic_spine "Dendritic spine")

A small, membranous protrusion from a neuron's dendrite that typically receives input from a single synapse of an axon. Spines are key sites for synaptic strength and plasticity.

[Depolarization](/wiki/Depolarization "Depolarization")

A change in membrane potential that makes the interior of a neuron more positive relative to the outside, often initiating an [action potential](/wiki/Action_potential "Action potential") if a threshold is reached.

[Descending neuron](/wiki/Descending_neuron "Descending neuron")

A neuron that originates in the brain or brainstem and projects downward through the spinal cord to influence motor circuits and autonomic function. Descending neurons are critical for voluntary movement, postural control, and reflex modulation. Major descending pathways include the corticospinal, reticulospinal, rubrospinal, and vestibulospinal tracts.

[Developmental neuroscience](/wiki/Developmental_neuroscience "Developmental neuroscience")

A branch of neuroscience focused on how the nervous system develops from embryonic stages through adulthood, including neurogenesis, synaptogenesis, and pruning.

[Diffusion tensor imaging](/wiki/Diffusion_tensor_imaging "Diffusion tensor imaging") (DTI)

A form of [magnetic resonance imaging](/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging "Magnetic resonance imaging") that maps the diffusion of water molecules in brain tissue to visualize white matter tracts and structural connectivity.

[Diencephalon](/wiki/Diencephalon "Diencephalon")

A region of the forebrain that includes structures such as the [thalamus](/wiki/Thalamus "Thalamus") and [hypothalamus](/wiki/Hypothalamus "Hypothalamus"). It plays a role in sensory relay, autonomic function, and endocrine regulation.

[Diffuse axonal injury](/wiki/Diffuse_axonal_injury "Diffuse axonal injury")

A form of traumatic brain injury where widespread lesions in white matter tracts occur due to rapid acceleration or deceleration, commonly associated with concussions and coma.

[Dissociation (neuropsychology)](/wiki/Dissociation_\(neuropsychology\) "Dissociation (neuropsychology)")

A situation where one cognitive function is impaired while another is preserved, often used to infer the independence of brain processes or structures.

[Dizygotic twins](/wiki/Dizygotic_twins "Dizygotic twins")

Also known as fraternal twins; twins that result from two separate eggs fertilized by two different sperm. Used in genetic studies to explore heritability in neurological traits.

[Dopamine](/wiki/Dopamine "Dopamine")

A neurotransmitter involved in reward, motivation, motor control, and mood regulation. Imbalances are associated with disorders such as [Parkinson’s disease](/wiki/Parkinson%E2%80%99s_disease "Parkinson’s disease"), [schizophrenia](/wiki/Schizophrenia "Schizophrenia"), and [addiction](/wiki/Addiction "Addiction").

[Dorsal column–medial lemniscus pathway](/wiki/Dorsal_column%E2%80%93medial_lemniscus_pathway "Dorsal column–medial lemniscus pathway")

A sensory pathway of the spinal cord that conveys fine touch, vibration, and proprioceptive information to the brain.

[Dorsal horn](/wiki/Posterior_horn_of_spinal_cord "Posterior horn of spinal cord")

A region in the gray matter of the spinal cord that receives and processes incoming sensory information from peripheral nerves.

[Dorsal root ganglion](/wiki/Dorsal_root_ganglion "Dorsal root ganglion")

A cluster of sensory neuron cell bodies located just outside the spinal cord, which transmit sensory signals to the [central nervous system](/wiki/Central_nervous_system "Central nervous system").

[Dorsal stream](/wiki/Dorsal_stream "Dorsal stream")

One of the two major visual processing pathways in the brain. Often referred to as the “where” pathway, it processes motion and spatial information.

[Downregulation](/wiki/Downregulation "Downregulation")

A decrease in the number or sensitivity of receptors in response to prolonged stimulation by a neurotransmitter or drug, reducing cellular response.

[Dualism](/wiki/Mind%E2%80%93body_dualism "Mind–body dualism")

A philosophical position asserting that the mind and body are fundamentally distinct entities. In neuroscience, it contrasts with monistic and physicalist views of consciousness.

[Dynein](/wiki/Dynein "Dynein")

A motor protein that moves along microtubules in the cytoskeleton, transporting cellular cargo toward the center of the cell. In neurons, dynein is important for retrograde axonal transport.

[Dysarthria](/wiki/Dysarthria "Dysarthria")

A motor speech disorder resulting from impaired control of the muscles used in speech, often due to neurological damage to the brainstem or [cerebellum](/wiki/Cerebellum "Cerebellum").

[Dyskinesia](/wiki/Dyskinesia "Dyskinesia")

Involuntary, erratic, or repetitive movements, often associated with long-term use of [dopaminergic](/wiki/Dopaminergic "Dopaminergic") drugs in [Parkinson’s disease](/wiki/Parkinson%E2%80%99s_disease "Parkinson’s disease").

[Dyslexia](/wiki/Dyslexia "Dyslexia")

A neurodevelopmental disorder characterized by difficulty with accurate and/or fluent word recognition, often linked to deficits in phonological processing and left hemisphere brain function.

[Dyspraxia](/wiki/Dyspraxia "Dyspraxia")

A developmental coordination disorder involving difficulty with motor planning and execution, affecting balance, fine motor tasks, and speech.

## E

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: E")\]

[EEG](/wiki/EEG "EEG") (Electroencephalography)

A non-invasive method of recording electrical activity in the brain using electrodes placed on the scalp. It is commonly used to study sleep, epilepsy, and cognitive processes.

[Effector](/wiki/Effector_\(biology\) "Effector (biology)")

A muscle, gland, or organ that performs an action in response to a neural signal, typically from the [motor neuron](/wiki/Motor_neuron "Motor neuron").

[Efferent nerve fiber](/wiki/Efferent_nerve_fiber "Efferent nerve fiber")

An axon that carries motor signals away from the [central nervous system](/wiki/Central_nervous_system "Central nervous system") to muscles or glands. Opposite of [afferent nerve fiber](/wiki/Afferent_nerve_fiber "Afferent nerve fiber").

[Electroconvulsive therapy](/wiki/Electroconvulsive_therapy "Electroconvulsive therapy") (ECT)

A psychiatric treatment in which seizures are electrically induced in anesthetized patients for therapeutic effect, often used for treatment-resistant depression.

[Electrode](/wiki/Electrode "Electrode")

A conductor used to detect or deliver electrical signals in neural recording or stimulation. Electrodes can be surface (non-invasive), intracranial, or implanted.

[Electromyography](/wiki/Electromyography "Electromyography") (EMG)

A technique for recording the electrical activity produced by skeletal muscles, often used in neuromuscular diagnostics and motor control studies.

[Electrophysiology](/wiki/Electrophysiology "Electrophysiology")

The study of the electrical properties of biological cells and tissues. In neuroscience, it often involves measuring voltage changes or currents in neurons.

[Endocannabinoid system](/wiki/Endocannabinoid_system "Endocannabinoid system")

A neuromodulatory system composed of endocannabinoids, receptors (e.g., CB1), and enzymes. It regulates processes such as appetite, pain, mood, and memory.

[Endocrine system](/wiki/Endocrine_system "Endocrine system")

A collection of glands that secrete hormones directly into the bloodstream. It interacts with the [nervous system](/wiki/Nervous_system "Nervous system") to regulate metabolism, stress, and growth.

[Endogenous](/wiki/Endogenous "Endogenous")

Originating from within an organism, tissue, or cell. In neuroscience, it typically refers to naturally occurring neurotransmitters or signaling molecules.

[Endorphins](/wiki/Endorphins "Endorphins")

Endogenous opioid peptides that bind to [opioid receptors](/wiki/Opioid_receptors "Opioid receptors") and act as natural painkillers and mood enhancers.

[Engram](/wiki/Engram_\(neuropsychology\) "Engram (neuropsychology)")

A theoretical physical trace or biological substrate of memory in the brain. The term is used in memory research to describe the neural changes underlying a specific memory.

[Enteric nervous system](/wiki/Enteric_nervous_system "Enteric nervous system")

A subdivision of the [autonomic nervous system](/wiki/Autonomic_nervous_system "Autonomic nervous system") that governs the function of the gastrointestinal system. Sometimes called the "second brain."

[Epinephrine](/wiki/Epinephrine "Epinephrine")

A hormone and neurotransmitter (also known as adrenaline) involved in the body’s fight-or-flight response. Produced in the [adrenal medulla](/wiki/Adrenal_medulla "Adrenal medulla").

[EPSP](/wiki/Excitatory_postsynaptic_potential "Excitatory postsynaptic potential") (Excitatory Postsynaptic Potential)

A depolarizing event in a postsynaptic neuron caused by the influx of positive ions, making the neuron more likely to fire an [action potential](/wiki/Action_potential "Action potential").

[Equilibrium potential](/wiki/Equilibrium_potential "Equilibrium potential")

The membrane potential at which there is no net flow of a particular ion across the cell membrane. It can be calculated using the [Nernst equation](/wiki/Nernst_equation "Nernst equation").

[Estrogen](/wiki/Estrogen "Estrogen")

A steroid hormone that, in addition to reproductive functions, affects brain structure, cognition, and neuroprotection, particularly in areas such as the hippocampus.

[Evoked potential](/wiki/Evoked_potential "Evoked potential")

A measured electrical response of the nervous system to a specific sensory, motor, or cognitive stimulus, commonly recorded with [EEG](/wiki/EEG "EEG").

[Excitability](/wiki/Membrane_potential#Cell_excitability "Membrane potential")

The ability of a neuron or muscle cell to respond to a stimulus and generate an [action potential](/wiki/Action_potential "Action potential").

[Excitotoxicity](/wiki/Excitotoxicity "Excitotoxicity")

Cell death caused by excessive stimulation by neurotransmitters such as [glutamate](/wiki/Glutamate "Glutamate"), leading to calcium overload and neuronal damage. It is implicated in stroke and neurodegenerative diseases.

[Executive function](/wiki/Executive_function "Executive function")

A set of cognitive processes including working memory, flexible thinking, and self-control. Primarily associated with the [prefrontal cortex](/wiki/Prefrontal_cortex "Prefrontal cortex").

[Exocytosis](/wiki/Exocytosis "Exocytosis")

The process by which vesicles release their contents outside the cell. In neurons, it refers to the release of [neurotransmitters](/wiki/Neurotransmitters "Neurotransmitters") into the synaptic cleft.

[Extracellular matrix](/wiki/Extracellular_matrix "Extracellular matrix")

A network of proteins and molecules outside cells

## F

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: F")\]

[Facial nerve](/wiki/Facial_nerve "Facial nerve") (Cranial Nerve VII)

A cranial nerve that controls the muscles of facial expression, conveys taste sensations from the anterior two-thirds of the tongue, and carries parasympathetic fibers to salivary and lacrimal glands.

[Facilitation](/wiki/Neural_facilitation "Neural facilitation")

A short-term increase in synaptic strength resulting from prior activity, due to residual calcium in the presynaptic terminal. It contributes to short-term plasticity and temporal coding.

[Fear conditioning](/wiki/Fear_conditioning "Fear conditioning")

A form of associative learning in which a neutral stimulus becomes associated with an aversive event. It is often used to study emotional memory and the role of the [amygdala](/wiki/Amygdala "Amygdala").

[Fiber tract](/wiki/Fiber_tract "Fiber tract")

A bundle of axons in the [central nervous system](/wiki/Central_nervous_system "Central nervous system") that connects different brain regions. Examples include the [corpus callosum](/wiki/Corpus_callosum "Corpus callosum"), [optic tract](/wiki/Optic_tract "Optic tract"), and corticospinal tract.

[Field potential](/wiki/Field_potential "Field potential")

An electrical potential recorded from a population of neurons, often reflecting the summed synaptic input to a region. Measured using [electroencephalography](/wiki/Electroencephalography "Electroencephalography") or implanted electrodes.

[Fight-or-flight response](/wiki/Fight-or-flight_response "Fight-or-flight response")

A physiological reaction to perceived threat or danger, involving the [sympathetic nervous system](/wiki/Sympathetic_nervous_system "Sympathetic nervous system") and hormones such as [epinephrine](/wiki/Epinephrine "Epinephrine") and [cortisol](/wiki/Cortisol "Cortisol").

[Forebrain](/wiki/Forebrain "Forebrain")

The largest part of the brain, consisting of the [cerebrum](/wiki/Cerebrum "Cerebrum"), [thalamus](/wiki/Thalamus "Thalamus"), and [hypothalamus](/wiki/Hypothalamus "Hypothalamus"). It is involved in complex behaviors, emotion, and cognition.

[Fornix](/wiki/Fornix_of_the_brain "Fornix of the brain")

A C-shaped bundle of white matter fibers that connects the [hippocampus](/wiki/Hippocampus "Hippocampus") to the [hypothalamus](/wiki/Hypothalamus "Hypothalamus") and other structures in the limbic system. Important for memory and emotional regulation.

[Free nerve ending](/wiki/Free_nerve_ending "Free nerve ending")

A type of unencapsulated nerve ending that detects pain, temperature, and crude touch. These are the most common type of cutaneous receptors.

[Frontal lobe](/wiki/Frontal_lobe "Frontal lobe")

A region of the [cerebral cortex](/wiki/Cerebral_cortex "Cerebral cortex") involved in decision-making, planning, motor function, problem-solving, and aspects of personality. Located at the front of each cerebral hemisphere.

[Frontal eye fields](/wiki/Frontal_eye_fields "Frontal eye fields")

A region in the frontal lobe involved in voluntary eye movements and visual attention. It plays a role in saccadic control and visual tracking.

[Functional connectivity](/wiki/Functional_connectivity "Functional connectivity")

A measure of how different brain regions interact during rest or tasks, often assessed using [fMRI](/wiki/FMRI "FMRI") or [EEG](/wiki/EEG "EEG") by examining correlations in activity over time.

[Functional magnetic resonance imaging](/wiki/Functional_magnetic_resonance_imaging "Functional magnetic resonance imaging") (fMRI)

A neuroimaging technique that measures brain activity by detecting changes in blood oxygenation (BOLD signal). It is used to study brain function in real time.

[Fusiform gyrus](/wiki/Fusiform_gyrus "Fusiform gyrus")

A brain region located in the temporal and occipital lobes involved in high-level visual processing, including facial recognition and object categorization.

[Fusiform face area](/wiki/Fusiform_face_area "Fusiform face area") (FFA)

A specialized part of the [fusiform gyrus](/wiki/Fusiform_gyrus "Fusiform gyrus") that responds selectively to faces. It is often studied in the context of prosopagnosia (face blindness) and visual expertise.

## G

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: G")\]

[GABA](/wiki/GABA "GABA") (Gamma-Aminobutyric Acid)

The primary inhibitory neurotransmitter in the central nervous system. It reduces neuronal excitability and is involved in sleep, anxiety, and motor control.

[Gag reflex](/wiki/Gag_reflex "Gag reflex")

A protective reflex that prevents choking, triggered by stimulation of the back of the throat. It involves cranial nerves IX and X and is used to assess brainstem function.

[Ganglion](/wiki/Ganglion "Ganglion")

A cluster of neuronal cell bodies located outside the central nervous system. Examples include the dorsal root ganglia and autonomic ganglia.

[Gap junction](/wiki/Gap_junction "Gap junction")

A direct electrical connection between adjacent neurons or glial cells formed by connexin proteins. Gap junctions allow ions and small molecules to pass rapidly between cells.

[Gene knockout](/wiki/Gene_knockout "Gene knockout")

A genetic technique used to inactivate or "knock out" a specific gene in an organism to study its function. Widely used in neuroscience to explore gene roles in brain development and behavior.

[Genetic predisposition](/wiki/Genetic_predisposition "Genetic predisposition")

An increased likelihood of developing a particular disease based on an individual's genetic makeup. In neuroscience, it is relevant to conditions like [schizophrenia](/wiki/Schizophrenia "Schizophrenia") and [Alzheimer's disease](/wiki/Alzheimer%27s_disease "Alzheimer's disease").

[Geniculate body](/wiki/Geniculate_nucleus_\(disambiguation\) "Geniculate nucleus (disambiguation)")

A relay structure in the thalamus. The lateral geniculate body is involved in visual processing; the medial geniculate body is involved in auditory processing.

[Gephyrin](/wiki/Gephyrin "Gephyrin")

A scaffolding protein that anchors inhibitory neurotransmitter receptors (GABA and glycine) at postsynaptic sites. Essential for synapse formation and stability.

[Glia](/wiki/Glia "Glia")

Non-neuronal cells in the nervous system that support, nourish, and protect neurons. Types include [astrocytes](/wiki/Astrocytes "Astrocytes"), [oligodendrocytes](/wiki/Oligodendrocytes "Oligodendrocytes"), [microglia](/wiki/Microglia "Microglia"), and [Schwann cells](/wiki/Schwann_cells "Schwann cells").

[Glia limitans](/wiki/Glia_limitans "Glia limitans")

A thin layer of astrocytic endfeet beneath the pia mater that forms part of the barrier separating the brain parenchyma from the cerebrospinal fluid.

[Glial scar](/wiki/Glial_scar "Glial scar")

A dense accumulation of glial cells, particularly astrocytes, that forms after central nervous system injury. It helps contain damage but can also inhibit axonal regeneration.

[Glioblastoma](/wiki/Glioblastoma "Glioblastoma")

A highly aggressive and malignant brain tumor arising from glial cells. It is the most common primary brain cancer in adults and often has a poor prognosis.

[Glucocorticoid](/wiki/Glucocorticoid "Glucocorticoid")

A class of steroid hormones released by the adrenal cortex in response to stress. They influence metabolism and have significant effects on memory and the [hippocampus](/wiki/Hippocampus "Hippocampus").

[Glutamate](/wiki/Glutamate "Glutamate")

The main excitatory neurotransmitter in the brain. It is essential for learning, memory, and synaptic plasticity, but excessive levels can cause [excitotoxicity](/wiki/Excitotoxicity "Excitotoxicity").

[Glutamatergic](/wiki/Glutamatergic "Glutamatergic")

Describes neurons or synapses that use [glutamate](/wiki/Glutamate "Glutamate") as a neurotransmitter. These are the majority of excitatory synapses in the brain.

[Glycine](/wiki/Glycine "Glycine")

An inhibitory neurotransmitter found primarily in the spinal cord and brainstem. It acts via [glycine receptors](/wiki/Glycine_receptors "Glycine receptors") and contributes to motor control and reflexes.

[Golgi cell](/wiki/Golgi_cell "Golgi cell")

A type of inhibitory interneuron in the [cerebellum](/wiki/Cerebellum "Cerebellum") that regulates input from mossy fibers to granule cells via [GABA](/wiki/GABA "GABA") release.

[Golgi stain](/wiki/Golgi_stain "Golgi stain")

A silver staining method that randomly labels a small subset of neurons in their entirety, allowing detailed study of neuronal morphology.

[G-protein-coupled receptor](/wiki/G-protein-coupled_receptor "G-protein-coupled receptor") (GPCR)

A large family of membrane receptors that activate intracellular signaling pathways through G-proteins. GPCRs are critical in neurotransmission, hormone signaling, and sensory perception.

[Granule cell](/wiki/Granule_cell "Granule cell")

A small neuron found in the [cerebellum](/wiki/Cerebellum "Cerebellum"), [olfactory bulb](/wiki/Olfactory_bulb "Olfactory bulb"), and [dentate gyrus](/wiki/Dentate_gyrus "Dentate gyrus") of the hippocampus. Granule cells play important roles in sensory and motor information processing.

[Granulomatosis with polyangiitis](/wiki/Granulomatosis_with_polyangiitis "Granulomatosis with polyangiitis")

An autoimmune disease that can affect the central nervous system, sometimes causing neuropathy, headaches, or stroke-like symptoms due to vascular inflammation.

[Grasp reflex](/wiki/Grasp_reflex "Grasp reflex")

A primitive reflex seen in infants where touching the palm induces the hand to close. Persistence in adults may indicate frontal lobe damage.

[Gray matter](/wiki/Gray_matter "Gray matter")

Brain tissue composed primarily of neuronal cell bodies, dendrites, and unmyelinated axons. It is responsible for information processing and synaptic transmission.

[Growth cone](/wiki/Growth_cone "Growth cone")

A dynamic, motile structure at the tip of a developing axon or dendrite that guides neuronal growth by sensing molecular cues in the environment.

[Growth factor](/wiki/Growth_factor "Growth factor")

A naturally occurring protein that stimulates cell growth, differentiation, and survival. Examples include [BDNF](/wiki/BDNF "BDNF"), [NGF](/wiki/Nerve_growth_factor "Nerve growth factor"), and [EGF](/wiki/Epidermal_growth_factor "Epidermal growth factor") in neural development and repair.

[Gustatory cortex](/wiki/Gustatory_cortex "Gustatory cortex")

The brain area responsible for processing taste information, primarily located in the insular and frontal opercular regions.

[Gyri](/wiki/Gyri "Gyri") (singular

[gyrus](/wiki/Gyrus "Gyrus"))

The raised ridges or folds of the cerebral cortex. They increase the brain’s surface area and are separated by [sulci](/wiki/Sulci "Sulci") (grooves).

[Gyrification](/wiki/Gyrification "Gyrification")

The process by which the cerebral cortex develops folds (gyri) and grooves (sulci) during brain development. Gyrification increases the surface area of the brain, allowing for greater cognitive capacity within the limited volume of the skull.

## H

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: H")\]

[Habituation](/wiki/Habituation "Habituation")

A form of non-associative learning in which an organism decreases or ceases its responses to a repetitive, harmless stimulus over time.

[Hallucination](/wiki/Hallucination "Hallucination")

A perception in the absence of external stimulus, such as hearing voices or seeing things that are not present. Common in conditions like schizophrenia or sensory deprivation.

[Hemispheres of the brain](/wiki/Hemispheres_of_the_brain "Hemispheres of the brain")

The two symmetrical halves of the brain—left and right—that control opposite sides of the body. Each hemisphere has specialized functions but works in coordination with the other.

[Hemispatial neglect](/wiki/Hemispatial_neglect "Hemispatial neglect")

A neuropsychological condition in which a person fails to attend to one side of space, usually the left, following damage to the right parietal lobe.

[Hering’s law of equal innervation](/wiki/Hering%27s_law_of_equal_innervation "Hering's law of equal innervation")

A principle in ocular motor control stating that yoked eye muscles receive equal neural signals to maintain coordinated binocular eye movements.

[Hertz](/wiki/Hertz "Hertz") (Hz)

A unit of frequency equal to one cycle per second. Used in neuroscience to describe brain wave activity, neural firing rates, and oscillations.

[Hindbrain](/wiki/Hindbrain "Hindbrain")

The posterior part of the brain, including the [medulla oblongata](/wiki/Medulla_oblongata "Medulla oblongata"), [pons](/wiki/Pons "Pons"), and [cerebellum](/wiki/Cerebellum "Cerebellum"). It regulates vital autonomic functions and coordinates movement.

[Hippocampus](/wiki/Hippocampus "Hippocampus")

A brain structure located in the medial temporal lobe, critical for memory formation, spatial navigation, and learning. Damage is associated with [anterograde amnesia](/wiki/Anterograde_amnesia "Anterograde amnesia").

[Histamine](/wiki/Histamine "Histamine")

A neurotransmitter involved in arousal, wakefulness, and inflammatory responses. Histaminergic neurons are primarily located in the hypothalamus.

[Histology](/wiki/Histology "Histology")

The microscopic study of tissues, including the structure of neural tissue such as neurons, glia, and blood vessels.

[Homeostasis](/wiki/Homeostasis "Homeostasis")

The regulation of internal conditions to maintain a stable physiological state. The [hypothalamus](/wiki/Hypothalamus "Hypothalamus") plays a key role in regulating temperature, hunger, and other homeostatic processes.

[Homunculus](/wiki/Homunculus "Homunculus")

A distorted representation of the human body based on the relative amount of cortex devoted to each body part, especially in the somatosensory and motor areas.

[Horizontal cell](/wiki/Horizontal_cell "Horizontal cell")

A type of retinal interneuron that connects photoreceptors and helps integrate and regulate input in the outer retina, contributing to contrast enhancement.

[Hormone](/wiki/Hormone "Hormone")

A signaling molecule released by glands that affects target cells in other parts of the body. Some hormones, like [cortisol](/wiki/Cortisol "Cortisol") and [estrogen](/wiki/Estrogen "Estrogen"), influence brain function and behavior.

[HPA axis](/wiki/HPA_axis "HPA axis") (Hypothalamic–Pituitary–Adrenal axis)

A major neuroendocrine system that controls stress response, digestion, mood, immune function, and energy usage through hormone release.

[Huntington's disease](/wiki/Huntington%27s_disease "Huntington's disease")

A genetic neurodegenerative disorder caused by a mutation in the HTT gene, leading to motor dysfunction, cognitive decline, and psychiatric symptoms. It involves degeneration of the [basal ganglia](/wiki/Basal_ganglia "Basal ganglia").

[Hydrocephalus](/wiki/Hydrocephalus "Hydrocephalus")

A condition in which excess [cerebrospinal fluid](/wiki/Cerebrospinal_fluid "Cerebrospinal fluid") accumulates in the brain's ventricles, leading to increased intracranial pressure. It may result from developmental issues, trauma, or infection.

[Hyperalgesia](/wiki/Hyperalgesia "Hyperalgesia")

Increased sensitivity to pain, often resulting from inflammation or damage to nociceptive pathways.

[Hyperpolarization](/wiki/Hyperpolarization_\(biology\) "Hyperpolarization (biology)")

A change in a cell’s membrane potential that makes it more negative, decreasing the likelihood of firing an [action potential](/wiki/Action_potential "Action potential").

[Hypoglossal nerve](/wiki/Hypoglossal_nerve "Hypoglossal nerve") (Cranial Nerve XII)

A cranial nerve responsible for controlling the muscles of the tongue. Damage can cause slurred speech and difficulty swallowing.

[Hypothalamus](/wiki/Hypothalamus "Hypothalamus")

A region of the brain below the thalamus that regulates homeostatic functions such as hunger, thirst, body temperature, and circadian rhythms. It controls the [pituitary gland](/wiki/Pituitary_gland "Pituitary gland") and links the nervous and endocrine systems.

## I

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: I")\]

[Iatrogenic](/wiki/Iatrogenic "Iatrogenic")

A condition unintentionally caused by medical treatment or diagnostic procedures. In neuroscience, may refer to nerve damage, seizures, or cognitive effects resulting from interventions.

[Ictal](/wiki/Ictal "Ictal")

Relating to or occurring during a seizure or convulsion. Commonly used in the context of [epilepsy](/wiki/Epilepsy "Epilepsy"), such as "ictal phase" versus "postictal phase."

[Imaging genetics](/wiki/Imaging_genetics "Imaging genetics")

A research field that combines brain imaging and genetics to investigate how genetic variation affects brain structure and function.

[Immunohistochemistry](/wiki/Immunohistochemistry "Immunohistochemistry")

A technique that uses antibodies to detect specific proteins or antigens in tissue sections. Frequently used to visualize neuronal and glial markers.

[Implicit memory](/wiki/Implicit_memory "Implicit memory")

Unconscious memory of skills and procedures, such as riding a bike or playing an instrument. Associated with brain structures like the [basal ganglia](/wiki/Basal_ganglia "Basal ganglia") and [cerebellum](/wiki/Cerebellum "Cerebellum").

[Incus](/wiki/Incus "Incus")

One of the three auditory ossicles in the middle ear that transmits sound vibrations from the [malleus](/wiki/Malleus "Malleus") to the [stapes](/wiki/Stapes "Stapes").

[Independent component analysis](/wiki/Independent_component_analysis "Independent component analysis") (ICA)

A computational method used to separate mixed signals into independent sources. Commonly used in [EEG](/wiki/EEG "EEG") and [fMRI](/wiki/FMRI "FMRI") to identify distinct brain activity patterns.

[Inferior colliculus](/wiki/Inferior_colliculus "Inferior colliculus")

A midbrain structure involved in auditory processing, particularly sound localization and reflexive responses to sound stimuli.

[Inferior olive](/wiki/Inferior_olive "Inferior olive")

A brainstem nucleus that sends climbing fiber inputs to the [cerebellum](/wiki/Cerebellum "Cerebellum") and plays a critical role in motor learning and coordination.

[Inhibitory postsynaptic potential](/wiki/Inhibitory_postsynaptic_potential "Inhibitory postsynaptic potential") (IPSP)

A type of synaptic potential that makes a neuron less likely to fire an [action potential](/wiki/Action_potential "Action potential"), usually by allowing chloride or potassium ions to enter the cell.

[Innervation](/wiki/Innervation "Innervation")

The supply of nerves to a specific body part. In neuroscience, it describes how neurons connect to and control muscles, glands, or other neurons.

[Input resistance](/wiki/Input_resistance "Input resistance")

A measure of how much a neuron's membrane resists incoming current. It influences how strongly the cell responds to synaptic inputs.

[Insular cortex](/wiki/Insular_cortex "Insular cortex")

A region buried within the lateral sulcus, involved in interoception, emotion, taste, and awareness of bodily states.

[Intelligence quotient](/wiki/Intelligence_quotient "Intelligence quotient") (IQ)

A standardized measure of cognitive ability. In neuroscience, research explores how brain structure and function relate to individual differences in IQ.

[Interneuron](/wiki/Interneuron "Interneuron")

A neuron that connects other neurons within a neural circuit, typically within the same brain region or spinal segment. Often inhibitory and essential for reflexes and signal modulation.

[Intracranial pressure](/wiki/Intracranial_pressure "Intracranial pressure")

The pressure inside the skull, exerted by brain tissue, blood, and [cerebrospinal fluid](/wiki/Cerebrospinal_fluid "Cerebrospinal fluid"). Abnormal levels can cause headaches, vision problems, or brain damage.

[Intracellular recording](/wiki/Intracellular_recording "Intracellular recording")

A technique used to measure the voltage or current inside a neuron, providing detailed information about membrane potential and ionic currents.

[Ion channel](/wiki/Ion_channel "Ion channel")

A pore-forming membrane protein that allows ions to pass in and out of neurons. Ion channels are critical for generating and propagating electrical signals.

[Ionotropic receptor](/wiki/Ionotropic_receptor "Ionotropic receptor")

A type of receptor that directly opens an [ion channel](/wiki/Ion_channel "Ion channel") upon neurotransmitter binding, allowing rapid synaptic transmission (e.g., [NMDA receptor](/wiki/NMDA_receptor "NMDA receptor"), [AMPA receptor](/wiki/AMPA_receptor "AMPA receptor")).

## J

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: J")\]

[Jacksonian seizure](/wiki/Jacksonian_seizure "Jacksonian seizure")

A type of focal seizure that begins in one area of the body (typically a finger or toe) and "marches" to adjacent areas, reflecting orderly spread of abnormal activity in the [motor cortex](/wiki/Motor_cortex "Motor cortex").

[James–Lange theory](/wiki/James%E2%80%93Lange_theory "James–Lange theory")

A historical theory of emotion proposing that emotions result from physiological responses to stimuli (e.g., "We are afraid because we tremble"). While outdated, it laid groundwork for modern affective neuroscience.

[Jaw jerk reflex](/wiki/Jaw_jerk_reflex "Jaw jerk reflex")

A stretch reflex elicited by tapping the chin, which causes the jaw to jerk upward. Used clinically to assess integrity of the trigeminal nerve and brainstem reflex circuits.

[Jitter](/wiki/Jitter "Jitter")

The variability in timing of neural spike trains or signal transmission, especially in motor units or brain-computer interface systems. High jitter can affect signal fidelity.

[JNK pathway](/wiki/JNK_pathway "JNK pathway") (c-Jun N-terminal kinase pathway)

A signaling cascade involved in stress responses, apoptosis, and neurodegeneration. Dysregulation of the JNK pathway is linked to [Alzheimer’s disease](/wiki/Alzheimer%E2%80%99s_disease "Alzheimer’s disease") and neuronal injury.

[Joubert syndrome](/wiki/Joubert_syndrome "Joubert syndrome")

A rare genetic disorder characterized by abnormal development of the cerebellar vermis and brainstem, leading to hypotonia, ataxia, abnormal breathing, and developmental delay.

[Juxtacrine signaling](/wiki/Juxtacrine_signaling "Juxtacrine signaling")

A form of cell-to-cell communication where signaling molecules are transmitted directly through contact, rather than being secreted. Important in neural development and [synaptogenesis](/wiki/Synaptogenesis "Synaptogenesis").

[Juxtarestiform body](/wiki/Juxtarestiform_body "Juxtarestiform body")

A bundle of fibers within the inferior cerebellar peduncle that connects the [vestibular nuclei](/wiki/Vestibular_nuclei "Vestibular nuclei") to the [cerebellum](/wiki/Cerebellum "Cerebellum"), involved in balance and eye movement coordination.

[Junctional complex](/wiki/Junctional_complex "Junctional complex")

A structural region where neurons, glia, or epithelial cells make close contact. In the nervous system, includes tight junctions and adherens junctions that help form the [blood–brain barrier](/wiki/Blood%E2%80%93brain_barrier "Blood–brain barrier").

## K

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: K")\]

[K-complex](/wiki/K-complex "K-complex")

A large, slow wave seen in [EEG](/wiki/EEG "EEG") during stage 2 of non-REM sleep. It is involved in memory consolidation and sensory processing during sleep.

[Kainate receptor](/wiki/Kainate_receptor "Kainate receptor")

A subtype of [ionotropic glutamate receptor](/wiki/Ionotropic_glutamate_receptor "Ionotropic glutamate receptor") involved in fast excitatory synaptic transmission. Kainate receptors play roles in learning and are implicated in seizure disorders.

[Kallmann syndrome](/wiki/Kallmann_syndrome "Kallmann syndrome")

A genetic condition characterized by delayed or absent puberty and an impaired sense of smell. It results from disrupted migration of olfactory and gonadotropin-releasing hormone neurons.

[Karyokinesis](/wiki/Karyokinesis "Karyokinesis")

The process of nuclear division during cell division. In the context of developmental neuroscience, it describes how neural progenitor cells divide in the developing brain.

[Karyotype](/wiki/Karyotype "Karyotype")

A visual profile of an organism’s chromosomes. In neuroscience, used in research or diagnostics for identifying chromosomal abnormalities linked to neurodevelopmental disorders.

[Kelvin–Voigt model](/wiki/Kelvin%E2%80%93Voigt_model "Kelvin–Voigt model")

A viscoelastic model used in computational neuroscience and biomechanics to describe tissue deformation, including brain tissue mechanics.

[Keratitis](/wiki/Keratitis "Keratitis")

Inflammation of the cornea, which may be linked to neural dysfunction in the trigeminal nerve or autonomic nervous system regulation.

[Kinesthesia](/wiki/Kinesthesia "Kinesthesia")

The sense of limb and body movement, closely related to proprioception. It is mediated by mechanoreceptors in muscles and joints and processed in the [somatosensory cortex](/wiki/Somatosensory_cortex "Somatosensory cortex").

[Kindling](/wiki/Kindling_model_of_epilepsy "Kindling model of epilepsy")

A process by which repeated sub-threshold electrical or chemical stimulation of the brain leads to progressively more intense seizures. Used as a model for [epilepsy](/wiki/Epilepsy "Epilepsy").

[Kleptomania](/wiki/Kleptomania "Kleptomania")

A psychiatric disorder characterized by recurrent urges to steal items not needed for personal use or value. Considered in neuropsychiatry as a type of impulse control disorder involving frontal lobe dysfunction.

[Klüver–Bucy syndrome](/wiki/Kl%C3%BCver%E2%80%93Bucy_syndrome "Klüver–Bucy syndrome")

A behavioral disorder caused by bilateral lesions of the [amygdala](/wiki/Amygdala "Amygdala") and [temporal lobes](/wiki/Temporal_lobes "Temporal lobes"), resulting in hyperorality, hypersexuality, and emotional blunting.

[Knock-in](/wiki/Knock-in "Knock-in")

A genetic technique where a gene is replaced or inserted at a specific locus, allowing researchers to study the function of specific mutations in neural systems.

[Knockout mouse](/wiki/Knockout_mouse "Knockout mouse")

A genetically engineered mouse in which one or more genes have been turned off through targeted mutation. Frequently used to study gene function in brain development and behavior.

[Korsakoff syndrome](/wiki/Korsakoff_syndrome "Korsakoff syndrome")

A chronic memory disorder caused by thiamine (vitamin B1) deficiency, most commonly due to chronic alcohol misuse. It involves degeneration of the [mammillary bodies](/wiki/Mammillary_bodies "Mammillary bodies") and medial [thalamus](/wiki/Thalamus "Thalamus").

[Krebs cycle](/wiki/Krebs_cycle "Krebs cycle") (Citric acid cycle)

A key metabolic pathway that provides energy to neurons. Although a general biology term, it is critical in neuroscience for understanding brain metabolism and mitochondrial function.

[Kymograph](/wiki/Kymograph "Kymograph")

An old device used to record changes in physiological activity over time, such as muscle contractions or nerve responses. Historically significant in neuroscience research.

## L

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: L")\]

[Lamina](/wiki/Lamina_\(anatomy\) "Lamina (anatomy)")

A layer of neurons in a neural structure. In the [spinal cord](/wiki/Spinal_cord "Spinal cord") and [cerebral cortex](/wiki/Cerebral_cortex "Cerebral cortex"), laminae are functionally and anatomically distinct.

[Lateral geniculate nucleus](/wiki/Lateral_geniculate_nucleus "Lateral geniculate nucleus") (LGN)

A relay center in the [thalamus](/wiki/Thalamus "Thalamus") that receives input from the [retina](/wiki/Retina "Retina") and sends visual information to the [visual cortex](/wiki/Visual_cortex "Visual cortex").

[Lateral inhibition](/wiki/Lateral_inhibition "Lateral inhibition")

A neural mechanism in which an excited neuron reduces the activity of its neighbors. Enhances contrast in sensory systems such as vision and touch.

[Lateral sulcus](/wiki/Lateral_sulcus "Lateral sulcus")

A deep groove in the cerebral cortex that separates the [frontal lobe](/wiki/Frontal_lobe "Frontal lobe") and [parietal lobe](/wiki/Parietal_lobe "Parietal lobe") from the [temporal lobe](/wiki/Temporal_lobe "Temporal lobe").

[Learning](/wiki/Learning "Learning")

The acquisition of new knowledge or skills through experience, study, or teaching. In neuroscience, learning is linked to synaptic plasticity and neural circuit changes.

[Learning](/wiki/Learning "Learning")

A neurobiological process through which experience induces lasting changes in the nervous system, leading to the acquisition or modification of behavior, knowledge, or skills.

[Lentiform nucleus](/wiki/Lentiform_nucleus "Lentiform nucleus")

A structure within the [basal ganglia](/wiki/Basal_ganglia "Basal ganglia") composed of the [putamen](/wiki/Putamen "Putamen") and [globus pallidus](/wiki/Globus_pallidus "Globus pallidus"), involved in motor control and learning.

[Lesion](/wiki/Lesion "Lesion")

Damage or abnormal change in brain tissue, caused by injury, stroke, disease, or surgery. Lesion studies are used to investigate brain function.

[Levodopa](/wiki/Levodopa "Levodopa")

A precursor to [dopamine](/wiki/Dopamine "Dopamine") used in the treatment of [Parkinson’s disease](/wiki/Parkinson%E2%80%99s_disease "Parkinson’s disease"). It crosses the blood–brain barrier and is converted to dopamine in the brain.

[Limbic system](/wiki/Limbic_system "Limbic system")

A group of interconnected brain structures involved in emotion, motivation, memory, and olfaction. Includes the [amygdala](/wiki/Amygdala "Amygdala"), [hippocampus](/wiki/Hippocampus "Hippocampus"), and [cingulate cortex](/wiki/Cingulate_cortex "Cingulate cortex").

[Lissencephaly](/wiki/Lissencephaly "Lissencephaly")

A rare brain malformation where the cerebral cortex lacks normal folds (gyri), leading to developmental delay and seizures.

[Long-term depression](/wiki/Long-term_depression "Long-term depression") (LTD)

A long-lasting decrease in synaptic strength following low-frequency stimulation. Thought to be important in motor learning and memory pruning.

[Long-term memory](/wiki/Long-term_memory "Long-term memory")

Stored information retained for extended periods—from hours to years. Includes declarative (explicit) and non-declarative (implicit) forms.

[Long-term potentiation](/wiki/Long-term_potentiation "Long-term potentiation") (LTP)

A long-lasting enhancement in signal transmission between two neurons that results from their synchronous activation. A major cellular mechanism underlying learning and memory.

[Low-threshold spikes](/wiki/Low-threshold_spikes "Low-threshold spikes")

A burst of action potentials generated by the activation of low-threshold, T-type calcium channels in response to a small depolarization from a hyperpolarized membrane potential. Commonly observed in thalamic neurons, low-threshold spikes enable rhythmic firing and are important for sleep-related oscillations and sensory signal gating.

[Lower motor neuron](/wiki/Lower_motor_neuron "Lower motor neuron")

A neuron that directly innervates skeletal muscle. Damage to these neurons causes flaccid paralysis, muscle atrophy, and loss of reflexes.

[LTP induction](/wiki/LTP_induction "LTP induction")

The process of initiating [long-term potentiation](/wiki/Long-term_potentiation "Long-term potentiation") through specific patterns of synaptic stimulation, such as high-frequency tetanic stimulation.

[Lucid dreaming](/wiki/Lucid_dreaming "Lucid dreaming")

A state during which the dreamer is aware that they are dreaming and may be able to exert control over the dream. Associated with increased [prefrontal cortex](/wiki/Prefrontal_cortex "Prefrontal cortex") activity.

[Luria–Nebraska Neuropsychological Battery](/wiki/Luria%E2%80%93Nebraska_Neuropsychological_Battery "Luria–Nebraska Neuropsychological Battery")

A standardized test battery used to assess a wide range of cognitive and motor functions. Based on the work of Alexander Luria, it helps localize brain dysfunction.

## M

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: M")\]

[Magnetic resonance imaging](/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging "Magnetic resonance imaging") (MRI)

A non-invasive imaging technique that uses magnetic fields and radio waves to visualize detailed structures of the brain and other tissues.

[Malleus](/wiki/Malleus "Malleus")

One of the three auditory ossicles in the middle ear. It transmits sound vibrations from the eardrum to the [incus](/wiki/Incus "Incus").

[Mammillary bodies](/wiki/Mammillary_bodies "Mammillary bodies")

Small round structures located in the hypothalamus involved in memory processing. Often damaged in [Korsakoff syndrome](/wiki/Korsakoff_syndrome "Korsakoff syndrome").

[MAPK/ERK pathway](/wiki/MAPK/ERK_pathway "MAPK/ERK pathway")

A cellular signaling cascade involved in growth, differentiation, and survival. It plays roles in synaptic plasticity and neurodegeneration.

[Medial geniculate nucleus](/wiki/Medial_geniculate_nucleus "Medial geniculate nucleus")

A relay center in the [thalamus](/wiki/Thalamus "Thalamus") for auditory information, sending signals from the inner ear to the [auditory cortex](/wiki/Auditory_cortex "Auditory cortex").

[Medial longitudinal fasciculus](/wiki/Medial_longitudinal_fasciculus "Medial longitudinal fasciculus")

A bundle of nerve fibers in the brainstem involved in coordinating eye movements and head motion, especially during the vestibulo-ocular reflex.

[Medulla oblongata](/wiki/Medulla_oblongata "Medulla oblongata")

The lower part of the [brainstem](/wiki/Brainstem "Brainstem"), responsible for autonomic functions such as respiration, heart rate, and blood pressure.

[Melatonin](/wiki/Melatonin "Melatonin")

A hormone produced by the [pineal gland](/wiki/Pineal_gland "Pineal gland") that regulates circadian rhythms and the sleep–wake cycle. Secretion is influenced by light exposure.

[Memory](/wiki/Memory "Memory")

The mental capacity to encode, store, and retrieve information. Subtypes include working memory, short-term memory, and long-term memory.

[Meninges](/wiki/Meninges "Meninges")

The three protective membranes—[dura mater](/wiki/Dura_mater "Dura mater"), [arachnoid mater](/wiki/Arachnoid_mater "Arachnoid mater"), and [pia mater](/wiki/Pia_mater "Pia mater")—that surround the brain and spinal cord.

[Merkel cell](/wiki/Merkel_cell "Merkel cell")

A mechanoreceptor found in the skin involved in the sense of touch, particularly pressure and texture. Communicates with sensory neurons.

[Mesencephalon](/wiki/Mesencephalon "Mesencephalon")

Also known as the [midbrain](/wiki/Midbrain "Midbrain"), this region of the brainstem is involved in motor control, vision, hearing, and alertness.

[Metabotropic receptor](/wiki/Metabotropic_receptor "Metabotropic receptor")

A type of neurotransmitter receptor that activates a second messenger system via G-proteins, resulting in slower but longer-lasting cellular effects.

[Microelectrode](/wiki/Microelectrode "Microelectrode")

A very fine electrode used to record the electrical activity of individual neurons.

[Microglia](/wiki/Microglia "Microglia")

The resident immune cells of the [central nervous system](/wiki/Central_nervous_system "Central nervous system"), responsible for clearing debris, responding to injury, and regulating inflammation.

[Middle ear](/wiki/Middle_ear "Middle ear")

The air-filled space containing the auditory ossicles (malleus, incus, stapes) that transmit sound from the outer ear to the inner ear.

[Midbrain](/wiki/Midbrain "Midbrain")

A portion of the [brainstem](/wiki/Brainstem "Brainstem") that includes structures such as the [tectum](/wiki/Tectum "Tectum") and [tegmentum](/wiki/Tegmentum "Tegmentum"). It plays roles in vision, hearing, motor control, and arousal.

[Mismatch negativity](/wiki/Mismatch_negativity "Mismatch negativity") (MMN)

An event-related potential detected via [EEG](/wiki/EEG "EEG") that reflects automatic brain response to deviations in auditory stimuli. Used in studies of perception and schizophrenia.

[Mitochondria](/wiki/Mitochondria "Mitochondria")

Organelles responsible for energy production in cells. In neurons, they play critical roles in metabolism, calcium regulation, and apoptosis.

[Molecular layer](/wiki/Molecular_layer_\(disambiguation\) "Molecular layer (disambiguation)")

The outermost layer of the [cerebellar cortex](/wiki/Cerebellar_cortex "Cerebellar cortex") and [cerebral cortex](/wiki/Cerebral_cortex "Cerebral cortex") (in some regions), containing relatively few neurons and mostly dendrites, axons, and synapses.

[Monoamine oxidase](/wiki/Monoamine_oxidase "Monoamine oxidase") (MAO)

An enzyme that breaks down monoamine neurotransmitters such as [dopamine](/wiki/Dopamine "Dopamine"), [serotonin](/wiki/Serotonin "Serotonin"), and [norepinephrine](/wiki/Norepinephrine "Norepinephrine"). Targeted by some antidepressants.

[Monosynaptic reflex](/wiki/Monosynaptic_reflex "Monosynaptic reflex")

A reflex arc that involves only one synapse between a sensory neuron and a motor neuron, such as the knee-jerk (patellar) reflex.

[Mossy fiber](/wiki/Mossy_fiber_\(cerebellum\) "Mossy fiber (cerebellum)")

A type of excitatory input fiber to the [cerebellum](/wiki/Cerebellum "Cerebellum") originating from the spinal cord and brainstem nuclei. They synapse onto granule cells.

[Motor cortex](/wiki/Motor_cortex "Motor cortex")

A region of the [frontal lobe](/wiki/Frontal_lobe "Frontal lobe") involved in planning, controlling, and executing voluntary movements.

[Motor neuron](/wiki/Motor_neuron "Motor neuron")

A type of neuron that transmits signals from the central nervous system to muscles or glands, enabling movement and action.

[Motor unit](/wiki/Motor_unit "Motor unit")

A motor neuron and all the muscle fibers it innervates. Varies in size depending on the precision required by the muscle.

[MRI spectroscopy](/wiki/In_vivo_magnetic_resonance_spectroscopy "In vivo magnetic resonance spectroscopy")

A specialized MRI technique that measures concentrations of certain brain chemicals or metabolites, aiding in diagnosis of tumors and metabolic disorders.

[Multisensory integration](/wiki/Multisensory_integration "Multisensory integration")

The process by which the brain combines information from different sensory modalities (e.g., vision and sound) to create coherent perception.

[Myelin](/wiki/Myelin "Myelin")

A fatty substance that insulates axons, increasing the speed and efficiency of [action potential](/wiki/Action_potential "Action potential") conduction. Formed by [oligodendrocytes](/wiki/Oligodendrocytes "Oligodendrocytes") in the CNS and [Schwann cells](/wiki/Schwann_cells "Schwann cells") in the PNS.

[Myelination](/wiki/Myelination "Myelination")

The process of forming a myelin sheath around axons, critical for normal neural function. Occurs progressively during development.

[Myoclonus](/wiki/Myoclonus "Myoclonus")

Sudden, involuntary muscle jerks or twitches. Can be physiological (e.g., sleep starts) or associated with neurological disorders.

## N

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: N")\]

[N-acetylaspartate](/wiki/N-acetylaspartate "N-acetylaspartate") (NAA)

A molecule found predominantly in neurons, often measured via [magnetic resonance spectroscopy](/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_spectroscopy "Magnetic resonance spectroscopy") as a marker of neuronal health and density.

[Narcolepsy](/wiki/Narcolepsy "Narcolepsy")

A neurological disorder characterized by excessive daytime sleepiness, sudden sleep attacks, and sometimes cataplexy. It involves dysfunction in the brain's regulation of sleep–wake cycles.

[Nasal cavity](/wiki/Nasal_cavity "Nasal cavity")

The air passageway behind the nose that houses the [olfactory epithelium](/wiki/Olfactory_epithelium "Olfactory epithelium"), which contains sensory neurons responsible for detecting odors.

[Neocortex](/wiki/Neocortex "Neocortex")

The largest part of the [cerebral cortex](/wiki/Cerebral_cortex "Cerebral cortex"), involved in higher-order brain functions such as sensory perception, motor commands, reasoning, and language.

[Neologism](/wiki/Neologism "Neologism")

A newly coined word or expression. In neuropsychology, may refer to nonsensical or made-up words produced by individuals with certain types of [aphasia](/wiki/Aphasia "Aphasia").

[Neostriatum](/wiki/Neostriatum "Neostriatum")

A subdivision of the [basal ganglia](/wiki/Basal_ganglia "Basal ganglia") comprising the [caudate nucleus](/wiki/Caudate_nucleus "Caudate nucleus") and [putamen](/wiki/Putamen "Putamen"). It is involved in motor control and reward processing.

[Neural crest](/wiki/Neural_crest "Neural crest")

A group of embryonic cells that gives rise to various structures, including peripheral neurons, glia, and parts of the face and skull.

[Neural oscillation](/wiki/Neural_oscillation "Neural oscillation")

Rhythmic or repetitive electrical activity in the central nervous system, often observed as brain waves in [EEG](/wiki/EEG "EEG") recordings.

[Neural plasticity](/wiki/Neural_plasticity "Neural plasticity")

The ability of the nervous system to change its structure and function in response to experience, injury, or development.

[Neural stem cell](/wiki/Neural_stem_cell "Neural stem cell")

A self-renewing progenitor cell capable of generating neurons and glial cells. Found in the developing brain and certain adult brain regions such as the [hippocampus](/wiki/Hippocampus "Hippocampus").

[Neurodegeneration](/wiki/Neurodegeneration "Neurodegeneration")

The progressive loss of structure or function of neurons, often leading to cell death. Seen in disorders such as [Alzheimer’s disease](/wiki/Alzheimer%E2%80%99s_disease "Alzheimer’s disease") and [Parkinson’s disease](/wiki/Parkinson%E2%80%99s_disease "Parkinson’s disease").

[Neurodevelopment](/wiki/Neurodevelopment "Neurodevelopment")

The biological processes through which the nervous system grows and matures, including neurogenesis, migration, synaptogenesis, and myelination.

[Neuroeconomics](/wiki/Neuroeconomics "Neuroeconomics")

An interdisciplinary field that combines neuroscience, psychology, and economics to study decision-making and reward processing in the brain.

[Neuroepithelial cell](/wiki/Neuroepithelial_cell "Neuroepithelial cell")

A type of cell in the developing neural tube that gives rise to neural progenitor cells, which later differentiate into neurons and glia.

[Neuroethology](/wiki/Neuroethology "Neuroethology")

The study of the neural basis of natural animal behavior, often involving model species and evolutionary comparisons.

[Neurofilament](/wiki/Neurofilament "Neurofilament")

A type of intermediate filament found in neurons, important for maintaining cell shape and axonal transport.

[Neurofibrillary tangle](/wiki/Neurofibrillary_tangle "Neurofibrillary tangle")

A pathological hallmark of [Alzheimer’s disease](/wiki/Alzheimer%E2%80%99s_disease "Alzheimer’s disease"), composed of hyperphosphorylated tau protein that disrupts normal neuronal function.

[Neurogenesis](/wiki/Neurogenesis "Neurogenesis")

The process by which new neurons are generated from neural stem or progenitor cells. Continues into adulthood in specific brain regions.

[Neuroglia](/wiki/Neuroglia "Neuroglia")

Also known as glial cells, these support, protect, and nourish neurons. Types include [astrocytes](/wiki/Astrocytes "Astrocytes"), [oligodendrocytes](/wiki/Oligodendrocytes "Oligodendrocytes"), and [microglia](/wiki/Microglia "Microglia").

[Neuroimaging](/wiki/Neuroimaging "Neuroimaging")

Techniques used to visualize the structure or function of the nervous system, including [MRI](/wiki/MRI "MRI"), [fMRI](/wiki/FMRI "FMRI"), [PET](/wiki/Positron_emission_tomography "Positron emission tomography"), and [EEG](/wiki/EEG "EEG").

[Neurologic examination](/wiki/Neurologic_examination "Neurologic examination")

A systematic clinical assessment of motor, sensory, reflex, and cognitive function to diagnose disorders of the nervous system.

[Neuromodulation](/wiki/Neuromodulation "Neuromodulation")

The physiological process by which a given neuron uses chemicals to regulate diverse populations of neurons. May be endogenous (e.g., [dopamine](/wiki/Dopamine "Dopamine")) or externally applied via electrical stimulation.

[Neuromodulator](/wiki/Neuromodulator "Neuromodulator")

A substance that alters the effectiveness of synaptic transmission without directly causing [excitatory](/wiki/Excitatory "Excitatory") or [inhibitory](/wiki/Inhibitory "Inhibitory") postsynaptic potentials. Examples include [serotonin](/wiki/Serotonin "Serotonin"), [acetylcholine](/wiki/Acetylcholine "Acetylcholine"), and [norepinephrine](/wiki/Norepinephrine "Norepinephrine").

[Neuron](/wiki/Neuron "Neuron")

A specialized cell in the nervous system that transmits electrical and chemical signals. Composed of a cell body, [axon](/wiki/Axon "Axon"), and [dendrites](/wiki/Dendrites "Dendrites").

[Neuropil](/wiki/Neuropil "Neuropil")

A dense network of interwoven nerve fibers, dendrites, and glial processes where synaptic activity occurs, commonly found in the gray matter of the brain.

[Neuroplasticity](/wiki/Neuroplasticity "Neuroplasticity")

See [neural plasticity](/wiki/Neural_plasticity "Neural plasticity").

[Neuroprosthetics](/wiki/Neuroprosthetics "Neuroprosthetics")

A field that develops devices that interface with the nervous system to restore lost function, such as cochlear implants or brain–computer interfaces.

[Neuropsychiatry](/wiki/Neuropsychiatry "Neuropsychiatry")

A medical field combining aspects of neurology and psychiatry to treat disorders with both neurological and psychiatric components, such as OCD or Tourette syndrome.

[Neuropsychology](/wiki/Neuropsychology "Neuropsychology")

The study of the relationship between brain function and behavior, often using cognitive testing and brain imaging to assess individuals with brain injury or disease.

[Neuroscience](/wiki/Neuroscience "Neuroscience")

The scientific study of the nervous system, encompassing a wide range of disciplines from molecular biology and anatomy to behavior and cognition.

[Neurosecretion](/wiki/Neurosecretion "Neurosecretion")

The release of signaling molecules (such as hormones) by specialized neurons, often into the bloodstream, as in the [hypothalamus](/wiki/Hypothalamus "Hypothalamus")–[pituitary gland](/wiki/Pituitary_gland "Pituitary gland") axis.

[Neurosteroids](/wiki/Neurosteroids "Neurosteroids")

Steroid molecules synthesized in the brain that modulate neuronal excitability and synaptic function, often via [GABA](/wiki/GABA "GABA") and [NMDA](/wiki/NMDA "NMDA") receptors.

[Neurotransmitter](/wiki/Neurotransmitter "Neurotransmitter")

A chemical substance released at the end of a neuron’s axon that transmits a signal across a synapse to another cell. Examples include [glutamate](/wiki/Glutamate "Glutamate"), [dopamine](/wiki/Dopamine "Dopamine"), and [serotonin](/wiki/Serotonin "Serotonin").

[Nodes of Ranvier](/wiki/Nodes_of_Ranvier "Nodes of Ranvier")

Regularly spaced gaps in the [myelin sheath](/wiki/Myelin_sheath "Myelin sheath") of axons that allow for saltatory conduction of [action potentials](/wiki/Action_potentials "Action potentials").

[Nociception](/wiki/Nociception "Nociception")

The sensory perception of pain, including detection of noxious stimuli and the signaling pathways that convey pain information to the brain.

[Nucleus accumbens](/wiki/Nucleus_accumbens "Nucleus accumbens")

A region of the [basal forebrain](/wiki/Basal_forebrain "Basal forebrain") involved in reward, pleasure, and addiction. Rich in [dopamine](/wiki/Dopamine "Dopamine") and a key component of the brain's reward circuit.

[Nystagmus](/wiki/Nystagmus "Nystagmus")

Rapid, involuntary eye movements that may be physiological or result from vestibular or neurological disorders.

## O

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: O")\]

[Obex](/wiki/Obex "Obex")

The point in the brainstem where the fourth ventricle narrows to become the central canal of the spinal cord. Used as an anatomical landmark in neuroanatomy.

[Occipital lobe](/wiki/Occipital_lobe "Occipital lobe")

The rearmost lobe of the cerebral cortex, primarily responsible for visual processing, including interpretation of color, motion, and spatial orientation.

[Ocular dominance](/wiki/Ocular_dominance "Ocular dominance")

A preference of neurons in the visual cortex for input from one eye over the other. Studied extensively in visual development and plasticity.

[Oculomotor nerve](/wiki/Oculomotor_nerve "Oculomotor nerve") (Cranial Nerve III)

A cranial nerve that controls most of the eye's movements, including those of the eyelid and pupil constriction. Damage may result in diplopia or ptosis.

[Olfaction](/wiki/Olfaction "Olfaction")

The sense of smell, mediated by receptors in the [olfactory epithelium](/wiki/Olfactory_epithelium "Olfactory epithelium") and processed in the [olfactory bulb](/wiki/Olfactory_bulb "Olfactory bulb") and related brain regions.

[Olfactory bulb](/wiki/Olfactory_bulb "Olfactory bulb")

The first brain structure to process olfactory (smell) information. Receives input from sensory neurons in the nasal cavity and sends projections to the [olfactory cortex](/wiki/Olfactory_cortex "Olfactory cortex") and limbic system.

[Olfactory cortex](/wiki/Olfactory_cortex "Olfactory cortex")

A region of the brain involved in odor identification and perception. Includes the piriform cortex and parts of the amygdala and entorhinal cortex.

[Olfactory nerve](/wiki/Olfactory_nerve "Olfactory nerve") (Cranial Nerve I)

The first cranial nerve, responsible for transmitting smell information from the nose to the brain.

[Oligodendrocyte](/wiki/Oligodendrocyte "Oligodendrocyte")

A type of glial cell in the [central nervous system](/wiki/Central_nervous_system "Central nervous system") that forms the [myelin sheath](/wiki/Myelin_sheath "Myelin sheath") around axons, enabling rapid signal conduction.

[Ontogeny](/wiki/Ontogeny "Ontogeny")

The development of an individual organism, particularly the growth and differentiation of the nervous system from embryo to adult.

[Operculum (brain)](/wiki/Operculum_\(brain\) "Operculum (brain)")

A part of the [cerebral cortex](/wiki/Cerebral_cortex "Cerebral cortex") that covers the insula. It includes portions of the frontal, temporal, and parietal lobes.

[Opsin](/wiki/Opsin "Opsin")

A light-sensitive protein found in photoreceptor cells of the retina. Plays a key role in the molecular detection of light in vision.

[Optic chiasm](/wiki/Optic_chiasm "Optic chiasm")

The point at which the optic nerves from both eyes partially cross, allowing visual information from each eye to be processed in both hemispheres of the brain.

[Optic nerve](/wiki/Optic_nerve "Optic nerve") (Cranial Nerve II)

A cranial nerve that transmits visual information from the retina to the [lateral geniculate nucleus](/wiki/Lateral_geniculate_nucleus "Lateral geniculate nucleus") of the [thalamus](/wiki/Thalamus "Thalamus").

[Optic tract](/wiki/Optic_tract "Optic tract")

The continuation of the [optic nerve](/wiki/Optic_nerve "Optic nerve") fibers after the [optic chiasm](/wiki/Optic_chiasm "Optic chiasm"); carries visual information to the thalamus and midbrain.

[Optogenetics](/wiki/Optogenetics "Optogenetics")

A technique that uses light-sensitive proteins to control the activity of genetically targeted neurons. Widely used in neuroscience research to study circuit function.

[Orbitofrontal cortex](/wiki/Orbitofrontal_cortex "Orbitofrontal cortex")

A prefrontal brain region involved in decision-making, reward processing, and emotional regulation. Dysfunction is linked to addiction and impulsivity.

[Organ of Corti](/wiki/Organ_of_Corti "Organ of Corti")

The sensory organ within the cochlea that contains hair cells and is responsible for converting sound vibrations into neural signals.

[Orientation column](/wiki/Orientation_column "Orientation column")

A vertical column of neurons in the [visual cortex](/wiki/Visual_cortex "Visual cortex") that respond to lines of the same orientation. Part of the brain’s system for processing visual features.

[Oscillation](/wiki/Oscillation "Oscillation")

Repetitive rhythmic activity in neurons or neural networks. Neural oscillations, such as [alpha waves](/wiki/Alpha_waves "Alpha waves") and [gamma waves](/wiki/Gamma_waves "Gamma waves"), are observed in brain activity recordings.

[Otolith organs](/wiki/Otolith_organs "Otolith organs")

Structures in the [vestibular system](/wiki/Vestibular_system "Vestibular system") (the utricle and saccule) that detect linear acceleration and head position relative to gravity.

## P

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: P")\]

[Pacinian corpuscle](/wiki/Pacinian_corpuscle "Pacinian corpuscle")

A type of rapidly adapting mechanoreceptor located deep in the skin, responsible for detecting vibration and pressure.

[Pain](/wiki/Pain "Pain")

An unpleasant sensory and emotional experience associated with actual or potential tissue damage. In neuroscience, pain is studied through nociception, pain pathways, and perception.

[Paralysis](/wiki/Paralysis "Paralysis")

The loss of voluntary muscle function, often caused by damage to the brain, spinal cord, or [peripheral nerves](/wiki/Peripheral_nerves "Peripheral nerves").

[Paraneoplastic syndrome](/wiki/Paraneoplastic_syndrome "Paraneoplastic syndrome")

A rare disorder triggered by an immune response to cancer that affects the nervous system. Can lead to sensory, motor, or cognitive symptoms.

[Parietal lobe](/wiki/Parietal_lobe "Parietal lobe")

A region of the [cerebral cortex](/wiki/Cerebral_cortex "Cerebral cortex") located near the top and back of the brain, involved in processing somatosensory information, spatial orientation, and body awareness.

[Parkinson’s disease](/wiki/Parkinson%E2%80%99s_disease "Parkinson’s disease")

A neurodegenerative disorder characterized by tremors, rigidity, bradykinesia, and postural instability. Caused by loss of [dopaminergic](/wiki/Dopaminergic "Dopaminergic") neurons in the [substantia nigra](/wiki/Substantia_nigra "Substantia nigra").

[Parvocellular pathway](/wiki/Parvocellular_pathway "Parvocellular pathway")

A visual processing stream originating in small ganglion cells of the retina, responsible for high-acuity and color vision. Projects to the [lateral geniculate nucleus](/wiki/Lateral_geniculate_nucleus "Lateral geniculate nucleus").

[Periaqueductal gray](/wiki/Periaqueductal_gray "Periaqueductal gray") (PAG)

A midbrain region involved in pain modulation, defensive behavior, and autonomic regulation. Contains opioid receptors and descending pain control pathways.

[Peripheral nervous system](/wiki/Peripheral_nervous_system "Peripheral nervous system") (PNS)

All nerves and ganglia outside the brain and spinal cord. It includes [sensory](/wiki/Sensory_neuron "Sensory neuron") and [motor neurons](/wiki/Motor_neurons "Motor neurons") as well as [autonomic nerves](/wiki/Autonomic_nerves "Autonomic nerves").

[Periventricular](/wiki/Periventricular_nucleus "Periventricular nucleus")

Located near or surrounding the brain’s ventricular system. Often used to describe regions affected in diseases like multiple sclerosis.

[PET scan](/wiki/PET_scan "PET scan") (Positron Emission Tomography)

A neuroimaging technique that uses radioactive tracers to measure metabolic processes, often used to study brain activity, neurotransmitter systems, and disease progression.

[Phantom limb](/wiki/Phantom_limb "Phantom limb")

A phenomenon in which amputees feel sensations, including pain, in a limb that is no longer present. Thought to result from cortical reorganization.

[Phenotype](/wiki/Phenotype "Phenotype")

The observable characteristics of an organism resulting from the interaction of its genotype with the environment. In neuroscience, may include behavioral traits, brain structure, or disease symptoms.

[Phineas Gage](/wiki/Phineas_Gage "Phineas Gage")

A famous historical case in neuroscience involving a railroad worker who survived a frontal lobe injury, providing early evidence of the brain’s role in personality and behavior.

[Photoreceptor](/wiki/Photoreceptor_cell "Photoreceptor cell")

A specialized neuron in the retina (rods and cones) that converts light into neural signals for visual processing.

[Pia mater](/wiki/Pia_mater "Pia mater")

The innermost of the three [meninges](/wiki/Meninges "Meninges") surrounding the brain and spinal cord. It closely follows the contours of the brain surface.

[Place cell](/wiki/Place_cell "Place cell")

A neuron in the [hippocampus](/wiki/Hippocampus "Hippocampus") that becomes active when an animal is in a specific location. Central to the brain’s internal spatial mapping system.

[Plasticity](/wiki/Neuroplasticity "Neuroplasticity")

The brain's ability to adapt and reorganize itself through experience, learning, or following injury. See also [neuroplasticity](/wiki/Neuroplasticity "Neuroplasticity").

[Pons](/wiki/Pons "Pons")

A structure in the [brainstem](/wiki/Brainstem "Brainstem") that relays signals between the [cerebrum](/wiki/Cerebrum "Cerebrum") and [cerebellum](/wiki/Cerebellum "Cerebellum"), and plays roles in sleep, respiration, and facial sensation.

[Postcentral gyrus](/wiki/Postcentral_gyrus "Postcentral gyrus")

The gyrus in the [parietal lobe](/wiki/Parietal_lobe "Parietal lobe") that contains the [primary somatosensory cortex](/wiki/Primary_somatosensory_cortex "Primary somatosensory cortex"), responsible for processing tactile information from the body.

[Posterior parietal cortex](/wiki/Posterior_parietal_cortex "Posterior parietal cortex")

A region involved in integrating sensory information for spatial awareness and movement planning. Important in reaching and grasping tasks.

[Postganglionic neuron](/wiki/Postganglionic_neuron "Postganglionic neuron")

A neuron in the [autonomic nervous system](/wiki/Autonomic_nervous_system "Autonomic nervous system") that extends from a ganglion to a target organ, influencing functions such as heart rate and digestion.

[Postsynaptic potential](/wiki/Postsynaptic_potential "Postsynaptic potential")

A change in membrane potential in a neuron following activation of synaptic receptors. Can be [excitatory](/wiki/Excitatory "Excitatory") (EPSP) or [inhibitory](/wiki/Inhibitory "Inhibitory") (IPSP).

[Prefrontal cortex](/wiki/Prefrontal_cortex "Prefrontal cortex")

The front part of the [frontal lobe](/wiki/Frontal_lobe "Frontal lobe") involved in decision-making, planning, social behavior, and personality. Highly developed in humans.

[Presynaptic terminal](/wiki/Presynaptic_terminal "Presynaptic terminal")

The end of an axon where neurotransmitters are released into the [synaptic cleft](/wiki/Synaptic_cleft "Synaptic cleft") following an [action potential](/wiki/Action_potential "Action potential").

[Primary motor cortex](/wiki/Primary_motor_cortex "Primary motor cortex")

A region in the [frontal lobe](/wiki/Frontal_lobe "Frontal lobe") responsible for initiating voluntary motor movements. Located in the [precentral gyrus](/wiki/Precentral_gyrus "Precentral gyrus").

[Primary somatosensory cortex](/wiki/Primary_somatosensory_cortex "Primary somatosensory cortex")

Located in the [postcentral gyrus](/wiki/Postcentral_gyrus "Postcentral gyrus") of the [parietal lobe](/wiki/Parietal_lobe "Parietal lobe"), it processes tactile and proprioceptive information from the body.

[Prion](/wiki/Prion "Prion")

A misfolded protein that can cause other proteins to misfold, leading to neurodegenerative diseases such as [Creutzfeldt–Jakob disease](/wiki/Creutzfeldt%E2%80%93Jakob_disease "Creutzfeldt–Jakob disease").

[Procedural memory](/wiki/Procedural_memory "Procedural memory")

A type of [long-term memory](/wiki/Long-term_memory "Long-term memory") for performing tasks and skills (e.g., riding a bike), often associated with the [basal ganglia](/wiki/Basal_ganglia "Basal ganglia") and [cerebellum](/wiki/Cerebellum "Cerebellum").

[Prosopagnosia](/wiki/Prosopagnosia "Prosopagnosia")

A neurological disorder characterized by the inability to recognize faces, often due to damage in the [fusiform face area](/wiki/Fusiform_face_area "Fusiform face area").

[Purkinje cell](/wiki/Purkinje_cell "Purkinje cell")

A large, GABAergic neuron located in the [cerebellar cortex](/wiki/Cerebellar_cortex "Cerebellar cortex"). It has extensive dendritic branching and is essential for motor coordination.

[Pyramidal cell](/wiki/Pyramidal_cell "Pyramidal cell")

A type of excitatory neuron found in the [cerebral cortex](/wiki/Cerebral_cortex "Cerebral cortex"), especially the [motor cortex](/wiki/Motor_cortex "Motor cortex"). Named for its pyramid-shaped soma.

[Pyramidal tract](/wiki/Pyramidal_tract "Pyramidal tract")

A major motor pathway that transmits signals from the [cerebral cortex](/wiki/Cerebral_cortex "Cerebral cortex") to the spinal cord, enabling voluntary movement.

## Q

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Q")\]

[QEEG](/wiki/QEEG "QEEG") (Quantitative Electroencephalography)

A technique that analyzes [EEG](/wiki/EEG "EEG") data using mathematical algorithms and statistical comparisons to identify abnormalities in brainwave activity. Used in research, clinical diagnostics, and neurofeedback.

[Quadrantanopia](/wiki/Quadrantanopia "Quadrantanopia")

A type of partial vision loss affecting a quarter of the visual field, usually resulting from damage to the [optic radiations](/wiki/Optic_radiations "Optic radiations") or [visual cortex](/wiki/Visual_cortex "Visual cortex").

[Quadriplegia](/wiki/Quadriplegia "Quadriplegia")

Paralysis affecting all four limbs, often due to cervical spinal cord injury or disease. Also known as tetraplegia.

[Quantal neurotransmitter release](/wiki/Quantal_neurotransmitter_release "Quantal neurotransmitter release")

The process by which neurotransmitters are released in discrete packets (quanta) from synaptic vesicles. Each quantum represents the contents of one vesicle.

[Quiescent state](/wiki/G0_phase "G0 phase")

A resting or inactive phase of a neuron or glial cell in which there is minimal metabolic or electrical activity. Relevant in stem cell research and neuronal excitability.

[Quinine](/wiki/Quinine "Quinine")

An alkaloid that blocks certain ion channels, including voltage-gated potassium channels. Used experimentally in neurophysiology and to treat malaria.

## R

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: R")\]

[Radial glial cell](/wiki/Radial_glial_cell "Radial glial cell")

A type of glial cell present during brain development that serves as a scaffold for migrating neurons and acts as a neural progenitor cell.

[Rasmussen's encephalitis](/wiki/Rasmussen_syndrome "Rasmussen syndrome")

A rare chronic inflammatory neurological disorder that typically affects one hemisphere of the brain, causing seizures, progressive weakness, and cognitive decline.

[Receptor potential](/wiki/Receptor_potential "Receptor potential")

A change in membrane potential in a sensory receptor cell in response to a stimulus. It may trigger [action potentials](/wiki/Action_potentials "Action potentials") in sensory neurons.

[Receptor tyrosine kinase](/wiki/Receptor_tyrosine_kinase "Receptor tyrosine kinase") (RTK)

A class of receptors involved in cell growth and differentiation. In neuroscience, RTKs mediate responses to neurotrophic factors like [BDNF](/wiki/BDNF "BDNF") and [NGF](/wiki/Nerve_growth_factor "Nerve growth factor").

[Reelin](/wiki/Reelin "Reelin")

A protein involved in regulating neuronal migration and positioning during brain development. Disruption is associated with lissencephaly and schizophrenia.

[Reflex](/wiki/Reflex "Reflex")

A rapid, automatic motor response to a stimulus, often mediated by the spinal cord without conscious brain involvement.

[Refractory period](/wiki/Refractory_period_\(physiology\) "Refractory period (physiology)")

A brief period following an [action potential](/wiki/Action_potential "Action potential") during which a neuron is less excitable or unable to fire another action potential.

[Relative refractory period](/wiki/Relative_refractory_period "Relative refractory period")

The phase during which a neuron can fire another [action potential](/wiki/Action_potential "Action potential"), but only with a stronger-than-normal stimulus. Follows the absolute refractory period.

[REM sleep](/wiki/REM_sleep "REM sleep") (Rapid Eye Movement sleep)

A sleep phase characterized by rapid eye movements, vivid dreams, muscle atonia, and heightened brain activity resembling wakefulness.

[Resting membrane potential](/wiki/Resting_membrane_potential "Resting membrane potential")

The baseline electrical potential across the neuron's membrane when it is not actively sending signals, typically around –70 mV in neurons.

[Reticular formation](/wiki/Reticular_formation "Reticular formation")

A network of nuclei in the brainstem involved in regulating arousal, attention, sleep–wake cycles, and autonomic functions.

[Retina](/wiki/Retina "Retina")

A thin layer of tissue at the back of the eye that contains [photoreceptors](/wiki/Photoreceptor_cell "Photoreceptor cell") and neurons responsible for detecting light and initiating visual processing.

[Retinotopy](/wiki/Retinotopy "Retinotopy")

An orderly representation of the visual field maintained throughout the visual pathways, including the [visual cortex](/wiki/Visual_cortex "Visual cortex").

[Retrograde amnesia](/wiki/Retrograde_amnesia "Retrograde amnesia")

A type of memory loss where individuals cannot recall past memories prior to a brain injury, while the ability to form new memories may be preserved.

[Retrograde transport](/wiki/Retrograde_transport "Retrograde transport")

The movement of materials from the axon terminal back to the cell body, typically via [dynein](/wiki/Dynein "Dynein") motors. Used for recycling vesicles and transporting signals.

[Rett syndrome](/wiki/Rett_syndrome "Rett syndrome")

A rare neurodevelopmental disorder, primarily affecting females, caused by mutations in the MECP2 gene. Characterized by developmental regression, motor problems, and seizures.

[Reuptake](/wiki/Reuptake "Reuptake")

The process by which neurotransmitters are reabsorbed by the presynaptic neuron after being released, terminating synaptic transmission.

[Reward system](/wiki/Reward_system "Reward system")

A group of brain structures involved in motivation, pleasure, and reinforcement. Includes the [ventral tegmental area](/wiki/Ventral_tegmental_area "Ventral tegmental area"), [nucleus accumbens](/wiki/Nucleus_accumbens "Nucleus accumbens"), and [prefrontal cortex](/wiki/Prefrontal_cortex "Prefrontal cortex").

[Rhinal cortex](/wiki/Rhinal_cortex "Rhinal cortex")

A region of the [medial temporal lobe](/wiki/Medial_temporal_lobe "Medial temporal lobe") that plays a key role in memory and object recognition. Includes the perirhinal and entorhinal cortices.

[Rhombencephalon](/wiki/Rhombencephalon "Rhombencephalon")

Also known as the hindbrain, this embryonic brain region gives rise to the [pons](/wiki/Pons "Pons"), [medulla oblongata](/wiki/Medulla_oblongata "Medulla oblongata"), and [cerebellum](/wiki/Cerebellum "Cerebellum").

[Ribosome](/wiki/Ribosome "Ribosome")

A cellular organelle responsible for protein synthesis. In neurons, local ribosome activity occurs in dendrites and axons, supporting synaptic plasticity.

[Rostral](/wiki/Rostral_\(anatomical_term\) "Rostral (anatomical term)")

A directional term meaning “toward the nose” or front of the brain. Often used in anatomical descriptions of the central nervous system.

[Ruffini ending](/wiki/Ruffini_ending "Ruffini ending")

A slow-adapting mechanoreceptor found in the skin that detects skin stretch and contributes to proprioception and object manipulation.

[RUNX1](/wiki/RUNX1 "RUNX1")

A transcription factor involved in sensory neuron differentiation and pain signaling. Active during embryonic development and in peripheral nervous system patterning.

## S

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: S")\]

[Saltatory conduction](/wiki/Saltatory_conduction "Saltatory conduction")

A process in which action potentials "jump" from one [node of Ranvier](/wiki/Node_of_Ranvier "Node of Ranvier") to the next along a myelinated axon, greatly increasing conduction speed.

[Schizophrenia](/wiki/Schizophrenia "Schizophrenia")

A severe mental disorder characterized by distortions in thinking, perception, emotions, and behavior. Associated with abnormalities in dopamine signaling and cortical connectivity.

[Schwann cell](/wiki/Schwann_cell "Schwann cell")

A type of [glial cell](/wiki/Glial_cell "Glial cell") in the [peripheral nervous system](/wiki/Peripheral_nervous_system "Peripheral nervous system") that forms the [myelin sheath](/wiki/Myelin_sheath "Myelin sheath") around axons, aiding in rapid signal transmission.

[Second messenger](/wiki/Second_messenger "Second messenger")

A molecule generated inside a cell in response to a neurotransmitter or hormone binding to a receptor. Examples include cAMP, IP₃, and Ca²⁺.

[Seizure](/wiki/Seizure "Seizure")

A sudden, uncontrolled electrical disturbance in the brain that can cause changes in behavior, movements, or consciousness.

[Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor](/wiki/Selective_serotonin_reuptake_inhibitor "Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor") (SSRI)

A class of antidepressants that increase serotonin levels in the synaptic cleft by blocking its reabsorption into the presynaptic neuron.

[Semantic memory](/wiki/Semantic_memory "Semantic memory")

A type of long-term memory involving general world knowledge, facts, and concepts, distinct from [episodic memory](/wiki/Episodic_memory "Episodic memory").

[Sensory neuron](/wiki/Sensory_neuron "Sensory neuron")

A neuron that responds to external stimuli such as light, touch, sound, or chemical signals and transmits sensory information to the central nervous system.

[Sensory cortex](/wiki/Sensory_cortex "Sensory cortex")

A region of the [cerebral cortex](/wiki/Cerebral_cortex "Cerebral cortex") involved in processing incoming sensory information. Includes the [primary somatosensory cortex](/wiki/Primary_somatosensory_cortex "Primary somatosensory cortex") and adjacent areas.

[Sensory gating](/wiki/Sensory_gating "Sensory gating")

A process by which the brain filters out redundant or unnecessary stimuli from all the sensory inputs, often studied in schizophrenia research.

[Sensory map](/wiki/Sensory_map "Sensory map")

An organized representation of sensory information in the brain. For example, the somatotopic map in the [primary somatosensory cortex](/wiki/Primary_somatosensory_cortex "Primary somatosensory cortex").

[Serotonin](/wiki/Serotonin "Serotonin")

A neurotransmitter involved in mood, sleep, appetite, and cognition. Dysregulation of serotonin systems is linked to depression and anxiety disorders.

[Short-term memory](/wiki/Short-term_memory "Short-term memory")

The capacity to hold a small amount of information in an active, readily accessible state for a short period of time. Often contrasted with long-term memory.

[Sleep spindle](/wiki/Sleep_spindle "Sleep spindle")

A burst of oscillatory brain activity visible on an EEG during Stage 2 [non-REM sleep](/wiki/Non-REM_sleep "Non-REM sleep"). Thought to play a role in memory consolidation.

[Somatic nervous system](/wiki/Somatic_nervous_system "Somatic nervous system")

A component of the [peripheral nervous system](/wiki/Peripheral_nervous_system "Peripheral nervous system") responsible for voluntary control of body movements via skeletal muscles.

[Somatosensory system](/wiki/Somatosensory_system "Somatosensory system")

The part of the sensory system concerned with the conscious perception of touch, pressure, pain, temperature, position, movement, and vibration.

[Spasticity](/wiki/Spasticity "Spasticity")

A condition of increased muscle tone (hypertonia) and exaggerated reflexes, often resulting from damage to upper motor neurons.

[Spatial memory](/wiki/Spatial_memory "Spatial memory")

The part of memory responsible for recording information about one’s environment and spatial orientation. Heavily dependent on the [hippocampus](/wiki/Hippocampus "Hippocampus").

[Spike-timing-dependent plasticity](/wiki/Spike-timing-dependent_plasticity "Spike-timing-dependent plasticity") (STDP)

A form of synaptic plasticity in which the timing of neuronal spikes determines whether synapses are strengthened or weakened.

[Spinal cord](/wiki/Spinal_cord "Spinal cord")

The part of the central nervous system housed within the vertebral column. It transmits signals between the brain and the rest of the body and controls reflexes.

[Split-brain](/wiki/Split-brain "Split-brain")

A condition resulting from surgical severing of the [corpus callosum](/wiki/Corpus_callosum "Corpus callosum"), often performed to treat severe epilepsy. Reveals the functional specialization of brain hemispheres.

[Stellate cell](/wiki/Stellate_cell "Stellate cell")

A star-shaped neuron, often [GABA](/wiki/GABA "GABA")\-ergic, found in regions like the [cerebellum](/wiki/Cerebellum "Cerebellum") and [somatosensory cortex](/wiki/Somatosensory_cortex "Somatosensory cortex").

[Stiles–Crawford effect](/wiki/Stiles%E2%80%93Crawford_effect "Stiles–Crawford effect")

A phenomenon in visual perception where light rays entering the eye near the center of the pupil are more effective in eliciting a visual response than those entering near the edge.

[Striatum](/wiki/Striatum "Striatum")

A major component of the [basal ganglia](/wiki/Basal_ganglia "Basal ganglia"), involved in movement, reward, and procedural learning. Composed of the [caudate nucleus](/wiki/Caudate_nucleus "Caudate nucleus") and [putamen](/wiki/Putamen "Putamen").

[Stroke](/wiki/Stroke "Stroke")

A condition where poor blood flow to the brain results in cell death. Symptoms depend on the affected brain region and may include paralysis, speech impairment, and memory loss.

[Subarachnoid space](/wiki/Subarachnoid_space "Subarachnoid space")

The area between the [arachnoid mater](/wiki/Arachnoid_mater "Arachnoid mater") and the [pia mater](/wiki/Pia_mater "Pia mater"), filled with cerebrospinal fluid that cushions the brain and spinal cord.

[Substantia nigra](/wiki/Substantia_nigra "Substantia nigra")

A midbrain structure involved in movement and reward. Degeneration of dopaminergic neurons here leads to [Parkinson's disease](/wiki/Parkinson%27s_disease "Parkinson's disease").

[Sulcus](/wiki/Sulcus_\(neuroanatomy\) "Sulcus (neuroanatomy)")

A groove on the surface of the cerebral cortex that separates adjacent gyri. Prominent sulci define major brain lobes and landmarks.

[Superior colliculus](/wiki/Superior_colliculus "Superior colliculus")

A structure in the midbrain involved in visual processing and control of eye movements. Part of the tectum.

[Supplementary motor area](/wiki/Supplementary_motor_area "Supplementary motor area")

A region of the medial frontal cortex involved in the planning and coordination of complex, internally generated movements. The SMA contributes to motor sequence initiation, bimanual coordination, and motor imagery.

[Synapse](/wiki/Synapse "Synapse")

The junction between two neurons (or between a neuron and another cell) where information is transmitted via neurotransmitters or electrical signals.

[Synaptic cleft](/wiki/Synaptic_cleft "Synaptic cleft")

The small space between the presynaptic and postsynaptic neurons across which neurotransmitters are released.

[Synaptic plasticity](/wiki/Synaptic_plasticity "Synaptic plasticity")

The ability of synapses to strengthen or weaken over time, based on activity levels. It underlies learning and memory.

[Synaptogenesis](/wiki/Synaptogenesis "Synaptogenesis")

The formation of new synapses between neurons. It occurs extensively during development and continues throughout life in certain brain regions.

[Synaptopathy](/wiki/Synaptopathy "Synaptopathy")

A disorder of synaptic function, often implicated in neurodevelopmental and neurodegenerative diseases such as autism and Alzheimer’s disease.

[Syringomyelia](/wiki/Syringomyelia "Syringomyelia")

A condition in which a cyst forms within the spinal cord, potentially damaging nerve fibers and leading to pain, weakness, and sensory loss.

## T

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: T")\]

[Tactile agnosia](/wiki/Tactile_agnosia "Tactile agnosia")

The inability to recognize objects by touch, despite intact somatosensation. Often results from lesions in the parietal lobe.

[Tardive dyskinesia](/wiki/Tardive_dyskinesia "Tardive dyskinesia")

A neurological disorder characterized by involuntary, repetitive movements, typically of the face or limbs. Often a side effect of long-term use of antipsychotic medications.

[Tau protein](/wiki/Tau_protein "Tau protein")

A microtubule-associated protein involved in stabilizing neuronal cytoskeletons. Abnormal tau aggregation is a hallmark of [Alzheimer's disease](/wiki/Alzheimer%27s_disease "Alzheimer's disease") and other tauopathies.

[Tectum](/wiki/Tectum "Tectum")

The dorsal part of the midbrain, containing structures such as the [superior colliculus](/wiki/Superior_colliculus "Superior colliculus") and [inferior colliculus](/wiki/Inferior_colliculus "Inferior colliculus"), involved in visual and auditory reflexes.

[Telencephalon](/wiki/Telencephalon "Telencephalon")

The anterior part of the forebrain that develops into the cerebral hemispheres. It contains the [cerebral cortex](/wiki/Cerebral_cortex "Cerebral cortex"), [basal ganglia](/wiki/Basal_ganglia "Basal ganglia"), and [limbic system](/wiki/Limbic_system "Limbic system") structures.

[Temporal lobe](/wiki/Temporal_lobe "Temporal lobe")

A lobe of the cerebral cortex involved in auditory processing, language comprehension, memory, and emotion.

[Thalamus](/wiki/Thalamus "Thalamus")

A deep brain structure that serves as a major relay center, transmitting sensory and motor signals to the cerebral cortex. It also plays a role in consciousness and alertness.

[Theta wave](/wiki/Theta_wave "Theta wave")

A type of brainwave oscillation in the 4–8 Hz range, typically associated with light sleep, meditation, and memory encoding processes.

[Third ventricle](/wiki/Third_ventricle "Third ventricle")

A narrow cavity in the brain located between the two halves of the [diencephalon](/wiki/Diencephalon "Diencephalon"), connected to the lateral and fourth ventricles and filled with [cerebrospinal fluid](/wiki/Cerebrospinal_fluid "Cerebrospinal fluid").

[Thoracic spinal cord](/wiki/Thoracic_spinal_cord "Thoracic spinal cord")

The portion of the spinal cord that corresponds to the thoracic vertebrae. It controls trunk muscles and part of the sympathetic nervous system.

[Tonic receptor](/wiki/Tonic_receptor "Tonic receptor")

A sensory receptor that continues to fire throughout the duration of a stimulus. Contrasts with [phasic receptors](/w/index.php?title=Phasic_receptors&action=edit&redlink=1 "Phasic receptors (page does not exist)"), which adapt quickly.

[Tonic-clonic seizure](/wiki/Tonic-clonic_seizure "Tonic-clonic seizure")

A type of generalized seizure involving an initial tonic phase of muscle stiffening followed by a clonic phase of rhythmic jerking. Often accompanied by loss of consciousness.

[Top-down processing](/wiki/Top-down_processing "Top-down processing")

Perception driven by cognition — the brain applies what it knows and expects to what it sees. In contrast to [bottom-up processing](/wiki/Bottom-up_processing "Bottom-up processing").

[Transcranial direct current stimulation](/wiki/Transcranial_direct_current_stimulation "Transcranial direct current stimulation") (tDCS)

A noninvasive brain stimulation technique that applies a low electrical current to the scalp to modulate neuronal excitability.

[Transcranial magnetic stimulation](/wiki/Transcranial_magnetic_stimulation "Transcranial magnetic stimulation") (TMS)

A noninvasive method to stimulate or inhibit brain activity using magnetic fields. Used in both research and treatment for depression and other disorders.

[Transduction](/wiki/Signal_transduction "Signal transduction")

The process of converting a physical stimulus (e.g., light, sound, touch) into an electrical signal in sensory receptors.

[Trigeminal nerve](/wiki/Trigeminal_nerve "Trigeminal nerve") (Cranial Nerve V)

The largest cranial nerve, responsible for sensation in the face and motor control of biting and chewing.

[Trophic factor](/wiki/Trophic_factor "Trophic factor")

A substance, such as [nerve growth factor](/wiki/Nerve_growth_factor "Nerve growth factor") (NGF), that supports the growth, survival, and differentiation of neurons.

[Tuberomammillary nucleus](/wiki/Tuberomammillary_nucleus "Tuberomammillary nucleus")

A small cluster of histaminergic neurons in the hypothalamus involved in arousal, attention, and the sleep–wake cycle.

[Two-point discrimination](/wiki/Two-point_discrimination "Two-point discrimination")

The ability to distinguish two closely spaced tactile stimuli as separate. Used to assess somatosensory function.

## U

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: U")\]

[Ultradian rhythm](/wiki/Ultradian_rhythm "Ultradian rhythm")

A recurrent biological cycle that occurs more than once in 24 hours, such as stages of sleep. In neuroscience, it refers to shorter rhythms regulating hormonal release, arousal, or sleep cycles.

[Unconditioned response](/wiki/Unconditioned_response "Unconditioned response")

In classical conditioning, an automatic, reflexive response to an unconditioned stimulus (e.g., salivation to food). Studied in behavioral neuroscience.

[Unconsciousness](/wiki/Unconsciousness "Unconsciousness")

A state in which an individual is not aware of themselves or their surroundings. Can result from head injury, anesthesia, or metabolic dysfunction.

[Uncus](/wiki/Uncus "Uncus")

A structure on the medial surface of the [temporal lobe](/wiki/Temporal_lobe "Temporal lobe"), part of the [parahippocampal gyrus](/wiki/Parahippocampal_gyrus "Parahippocampal gyrus"). It is involved in olfaction and is adjacent to the [amygdala](/wiki/Amygdala "Amygdala").

[Undershoot](/wiki/Afterhyperpolarization "Afterhyperpolarization")

The hyperpolarizing phase following an [action potential](/wiki/Action_potential "Action potential") where membrane potential temporarily becomes more negative than resting potential.

[Unilateral neglect](/wiki/Unilateral_neglect "Unilateral neglect")

A neuropsychological condition following damage (typically to the right [parietal lobe](/wiki/Parietal_lobe "Parietal lobe")) in which patients ignore stimuli on one side of space.

[Unipolar depression](/wiki/Unipolar_depression "Unipolar depression")

A mood disorder characterized by persistent low mood, lack of energy, and anhedonia, without manic episodes. Associated with altered activity in the prefrontal cortex and limbic structures.

[Unipolar neuron](/wiki/Unipolar_neuron "Unipolar neuron")

A type of neuron with a single process extending from the cell body. Common in invertebrates and found in human sensory neurons.

[Upper motor neuron](/wiki/Upper_motor_neuron "Upper motor neuron")

A neuron originating in the cerebral cortex or brainstem that transmits motor signals to [lower motor neurons](/wiki/Lower_motor_neurons "Lower motor neurons"). Damage leads to spasticity, hyperreflexia, and weakness.

[Upregulation](/wiki/Upregulation "Upregulation")

An increase in the number or sensitivity of receptors on a neuron, often in response to reduced stimulation or drug exposure. A mechanism of plasticity.

[Utricle](/wiki/Utricle_\(ear\) "Utricle (ear)")

A structure within the [vestibular system](/wiki/Vestibular_system "Vestibular system") that detects linear acceleration and head position relative to gravity. Works in tandem with the [saccule](/wiki/Saccule "Saccule").

[Uveitis](/wiki/Uveitis "Uveitis")

Inflammation of the uveal tract (including the iris, ciliary body, and choroid), which can cause visual disturbances and is sometimes associated with autoimmune neurological disorders.

## V

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: V")\]

[Vagus nerve](/wiki/Vagus_nerve "Vagus nerve")

Cranial nerve X, responsible for parasympathetic control of the heart, lungs, and digestive tract. It also conveys sensory information from the viscera to the brainstem.

[Vasopressin](/wiki/Vasopressin "Vasopressin")

Also known as antidiuretic hormone (ADH), it regulates water balance and blood pressure. In the brain, vasopressin influences social behavior and memory.

[Ventral](/wiki/Ventral "Ventral")

A directional term meaning "toward the front" in the spinal cord or "toward the bottom" in the brain. Opposite of [dorsal](/wiki/Dorsal_\(anatomy\) "Dorsal (anatomy)").

[Ventral horn](/wiki/Anterior_horn_of_spinal_cord "Anterior horn of spinal cord")

The anterior portion of the spinal cord’s gray matter, containing motor neurons that project to skeletal muscles.

[Ventral stream](/wiki/Ventral_stream "Ventral stream")

A visual processing pathway ("what" pathway) that travels from the occipital lobe to the temporal lobe and is involved in object recognition and form representation.

[Ventral tegmental area](/wiki/Ventral_tegmental_area "Ventral tegmental area") (VTA)

A midbrain structure containing dopaminergic neurons that project to the [nucleus accumbens](/wiki/Nucleus_accumbens "Nucleus accumbens") and [prefrontal cortex](/wiki/Prefrontal_cortex "Prefrontal cortex"), central to reward and motivation.

[Ventricles](/wiki/Ventricular_system "Ventricular system")

A set of interconnected cavities in the brain filled with [cerebrospinal fluid](/wiki/Cerebrospinal_fluid "Cerebrospinal fluid") (CSF), which cushions the brain and maintains intracranial pressure.

[Vestibular system](/wiki/Vestibular_system "Vestibular system")

The sensory system that contributes to balance, spatial orientation, and coordination of head and eye movements. Includes the semicircular canals and otolith organs.

[Vestibulo-ocular reflex](/wiki/Vestibulo-ocular_reflex "Vestibulo-ocular reflex") (VOR)

A reflex that stabilizes vision by producing eye movements in the opposite direction of head movement, allowing steady gaze.

[Vesicle](/wiki/Synaptic_vesicle "Synaptic vesicle")

A small membrane-bound structure within neurons that stores and releases neurotransmitters into the synaptic cleft during synaptic transmission.

[Vesicular transport](/wiki/Vesicular_transport "Vesicular transport")

The process by which neurotransmitters are packaged into synaptic vesicles and transported to the axon terminal for release.

[Visual cortex](/wiki/Visual_cortex "Visual cortex")

The region of the cerebral cortex responsible for processing visual information. Includes primary visual cortex (V1) and surrounding higher-order visual areas.

[Visual field](/wiki/Visual_field "Visual field")

The entire area that can be seen when the eyes are fixed in one position. Deficits in specific areas of the visual field are used to localize brain lesions.

[Volition](/wiki/Volition_\(psychology\) "Volition (psychology)")

The cognitive process of making decisions or initiating voluntary movements. Involves the [prefrontal cortex](/wiki/Prefrontal_cortex "Prefrontal cortex") and [supplementary motor area](/wiki/Supplementary_motor_area "Supplementary motor area").

[Voxel-based morphometry](/wiki/Voxel-based_morphometry "Voxel-based morphometry")

A neuroimaging analysis technique that compares local concentrations of brain tissue (e.g., gray matter) across individuals using MRI data.

## W

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: W")\]

[Wada test](/wiki/Wada_test "Wada test")

A procedure used to determine language and memory functions in each hemisphere of the brain. It involves anesthetizing one hemisphere at a time, usually with sodium amobarbital, and is commonly performed prior to epilepsy surgery.

[Wallerian degeneration](/wiki/Wallerian_degeneration "Wallerian degeneration")

A process of degeneration that occurs in an axon distal to the site of injury. It is a key feature of nerve damage in both the central and peripheral nervous systems.

[Waxy flexibility](/wiki/Waxy_flexibility "Waxy flexibility")

A psychomotor symptom often associated with catatonia and schizophrenia in which a patient’s limbs remain in a fixed position after being moved by someone else.

[Weber–Fechner law](/wiki/Weber%E2%80%93Fechner_law "Weber–Fechner law")

A principle in sensory neuroscience that describes the relationship between the magnitude of a physical stimulus and the perceived intensity. Suggests logarithmic scaling of sensation.

[Wernicke's area](/wiki/Wernicke%27s_area "Wernicke's area")

A region of the posterior superior temporal gyrus in the dominant hemisphere, involved in language comprehension. Damage here causes Wernicke’s aphasia, which impairs understanding of spoken and written language.

[Wernicke's encephalopathy](/wiki/Wernicke%27s_encephalopathy "Wernicke's encephalopathy")

A neurological condition caused by thiamine deficiency, typically seen in chronic alcoholics. It manifests with a triad of symptoms: confusion, ataxia, and ophthalmoplegia.

[West syndrome](/wiki/West_syndrome "West syndrome")

A severe form of epilepsy in infancy characterized by infantile spasms, developmental regression, and a distinctive EEG pattern called hypsarrhythmia.

[White matter](/wiki/White_matter "White matter")

Tissue in the brain and spinal cord composed primarily of myelinated axons, facilitating communication between gray matter regions. Plays a central role in signal transmission speed and integration.

[White matter hyperintensities](/wiki/White_matter_hyperintensities "White matter hyperintensities")

Lesions seen on MRI as bright spots in the brain's white matter, often associated with aging, vascular disease, and cognitive decline.

[Wilson's disease](/wiki/Wilson%27s_disease "Wilson's disease")

A rare genetic disorder involving impaired copper metabolism that leads to copper buildup in the brain, liver, and other tissues. Neurological symptoms include tremors, dystonia, and psychiatric changes.

[Wolfram syndrome](/wiki/Wolfram_syndrome "Wolfram syndrome")

A rare genetic disorder involving diabetes insipidus, diabetes mellitus, optic atrophy, and deafness (DIDMOAD). Associated with neurodegeneration and mitochondrial dysfunction.

[Working memory](/wiki/Working_memory "Working memory")

A form of short-term memory that allows temporary storage and manipulation of information necessary for tasks like reasoning and comprehension. Often localized to the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex.

[Wrist drop](/wiki/Wrist_drop "Wrist drop")

A neurological condition caused by radial nerve damage, resulting in an inability to extend the wrist and fingers. Often associated with nerve compression or trauma.

## X

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: X")\]

[X-chromosome inactivation](/wiki/X-chromosome_inactivation "X-chromosome inactivation")

A process in female mammals where one of the two X chromosomes is randomly silenced in each cell to balance gene dosage with males. Some neurological disorders, such as Rett syndrome, are influenced by X-inactivation patterns.

[X-linked adrenoleukodystrophy](/wiki/X-linked_adrenoleukodystrophy "X-linked adrenoleukodystrophy")

A genetic disorder affecting the breakdown of very-long-chain fatty acids, leading to their accumulation in the brain and adrenal glands. It causes progressive demyelination and adrenal insufficiency.

[X-linked intellectual disability](/wiki/X-linked_intellectual_disability "X-linked intellectual disability")

A group of inherited cognitive disorders associated with mutations on the X chromosome. Examples include Fragile X syndrome and MECP2 duplication syndrome.

[Xenon (anesthetic)](/wiki/Xenon#Anesthesia "Xenon")

A noble gas with neuroprotective and anesthetic properties. It has been studied for its NMDA receptor antagonism and minimal side effects on brain function.

[Xenotransplantation](/wiki/Xenotransplantation "Xenotransplantation")

The transplantation of cells or tissues from one species to another for research or therapeutic purposes. In neuroscience, fetal pig or rat neural cells may be studied in primate models to investigate brain repair.

[Xerostomia](/wiki/Xerostomia "Xerostomia")

A condition of dry mouth that may result from neurological disorders affecting autonomic control of salivary glands, such as Parkinson's disease or side effects of anticholinergic drugs.

## Y

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: Y")\]

[Yawn](/wiki/Yawn "Yawn")

A reflex consisting of the simultaneous inhalation of air and stretching of the eardrums, followed by exhalation. Neurologically, yawning is linked to arousal, social communication, and thermoregulation of the brain.

[Yerkes–Dodson law](/wiki/Yerkes%E2%80%93Dodson_law "Yerkes–Dodson law")

A psychological principle describing the relationship between arousal and performance, suggesting that moderate arousal optimizes cognitive and motor performance. Widely referenced in neuroscience of stress and motivation.

[Y chromosome microdeletion](/wiki/Y_chromosome_microdeletion "Y chromosome microdeletion") (YCM)

A genetic deletion on the Y chromosome that can affect brain development and function. Though rare, YCMs have been linked to neurodevelopmental conditions and fertility-related hormonal regulation.

[Yohimbine](/wiki/Yohimbine "Yohimbine")

A plant-derived alkaloid and alpha-2 adrenergic receptor antagonist. In neuroscience research, it is used to model anxiety and stress responses in animals and humans.

[Young–Helmholtz theory](/wiki/Young%E2%80%93Helmholtz_theory "Young–Helmholtz theory")

A theory of trichromatic color vision proposing that the human eye perceives color through the relative activity of three types of photoreceptors sensitive to red, green, and blue light. Supported by modern neuroscience of vision.

## Z

\[[edit](/w/index.php?title=Glossary_of_neuroscience&action=edit&section=26 "Edit section: Z")\]

[Z-drugs](/wiki/Z-drugs "Z-drugs")

A class of non-benzodiazepine medications (e.g., zolpidem, zaleplon, eszopiclone) used to treat insomnia. They act as GABA\_A receptor agonists and influence sleep architecture.

[Zellweger syndrome](/wiki/Zellweger_syndrome "Zellweger syndrome")

A rare congenital disorder caused by peroxisomal dysfunction, affecting brain development, myelination, and motor function. Symptoms include hypotonia, seizures, and impaired neuronal migration.

[Zona incerta](/wiki/Zona_incerta "Zona incerta")

A small region of gray matter in the subthalamus involved in sensorimotor integration, attention, and limbic functions. Its precise role remains under investigation.

[Zygosity](/wiki/Zygosity "Zygosity")

The genetic relationship between alleles. In neuroscience research, zygosity is important in twin studies exploring heritability of traits like intelligence, brain volume, and psychiatric disorders.
